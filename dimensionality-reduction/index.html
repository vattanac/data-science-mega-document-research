1. Why Reduce Dimensions?

Real-world data is high-dimensional: images have thousands of pixels, text has thousands of words, genomics has thousands of genes. Working directly in high dimensions is slow, memory-intensive, and often impossible to visualize.

Analogy: Shadow Casting

Imagine a complex 3D sculpture. You can't see the whole thing at once, but shining a light casts a 2D shadow on the wall that captures the shape's essence. Dimensionality reduction is like choosing the best angle to shine the light — keeping the most informative "shadow" of your high-dimensional data.

Visualization

We can only see in 2D/3D. Reducing 100 features to 2 lets us plot and see clusters, outliers, and patterns.

Speed

Fewer features = faster training. Going from 10,000 to 50 features can make models 100x faster.

Noise Removal

Many features are noise. Keeping only the most important dimensions removes noise and can improve model accuracy.

Curse of Dimensionality

In high dimensions, distances become meaningless, data becomes sparse, and models need exponentially more data.

2. The Curse of Dimensionality

As dimensions grow, counterintuitive things happen:

• In 1D, 10 evenly-spaced points cover a line well. In 100D, you'd need 10¹⁰⁰ points for the same coverage.

• In high dimensions, all points become roughly equidistant — distances lose meaning.

• The volume of a hypersphere shrinks to near-zero compared to the hypercube around it.

• Most data points end up on the "surface" — there's no interior.

The Good News

Real data rarely actually lives in all its dimensions. A dataset with 1000 features often truly varies along only 10-50 directions — the rest is noise or redundancy. Dimensionality reduction finds those true underlying directions. This is called the intrinsic dimensionality of the data.

3. PCA — Principal Component Analysis

PCA is the most widely used dimensionality reduction method. It finds the directions (principal components) along which the data varies the most, then projects the data onto those directions.

PCA Core Idea
Find eigenvectors of the covariance matrix → these are the principal components → project data onto top-k
Analogy: Pancake Stack

Imagine your data is a pancake stack (flat disk in 3D). PCA notices that most variation is in two directions (the flat surface) and almost none in the third (thickness). It says "just keep the flat directions" and compresses your 3D pancake into a 2D circle — losing almost nothing.

Key Properties of PCA

• Linear method: only finds straight axes (can't capture curved structures)

• Orthogonal: principal components are perpendicular to each other

• Ordered by variance: PC1 captures most variance, PC2 second-most, etc.

• Deterministic: same data always gives the same result

• Invertible: you can reconstruct (approximately) from reduced dimensions

• Fast: O(min(n²d, nd²)) — handles large datasets well

4. PCA Step by Step

  1. Center the data: Subtract the mean of each feature. Now the data is centered at the origin. (Optionally standardize if features have different scales.)
  2. Compute covariance matrix: C = (1/n) Xᵀ X. This n×n matrix captures how each pair of features varies together.
  3. Find eigenvalues & eigenvectors: Solve C·v = λ·v. The eigenvectors are the principal component directions. The eigenvalues tell you how much variance each direction captures.
  4. Sort by eigenvalue: Rank eigenvectors from largest λ (most variance) to smallest.
  5. Choose k components: Keep top-k eigenvectors. Common rule: keep enough to explain 90-95% of total variance.
  6. Project: Multiply data by the k eigenvectors: X_reduced = X · V_k. Your data is now k-dimensional.

How Many Components to Keep?

• Explained variance ratio: Plot cumulative variance vs. number of components. Pick the "elbow" where adding more components barely helps.

• Kaiser rule: Keep components with eigenvalue > 1 (above average variance).

• 90-95% threshold: Keep enough to explain 90-95% of total variance.

6. t-SNE — Seeing Clusters

t-Distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding is a nonlinear method designed specifically for visualization (typically to 2D).

t-SNE Core Idea
Preserve "neighborhood" probabilities: nearby points in high-D should stay nearby in low-D

How It Works (Simplified)

  1. Compute pairwise similarities in high-D: For each point, compute Gaussian probabilities over neighbors (nearby = high probability). The perplexity parameter controls effective neighborhood size.
  2. Initialize random low-D embedding: Place points randomly in 2D.
  3. Compute pairwise similarities in low-D: Use a Student-t distribution (heavier tails than Gaussian).
  4. Minimize KL divergence: Use gradient descent to make the low-D probabilities match the high-D ones. Points that were neighbors in high-D get pulled together in 2D.
Why Student-t in Low-D?

The "crowding problem": in 2D there's less room to place points than in high-D. The heavy-tailed t-distribution allows distant points to be even further apart in 2D, making room for nearby points to stay close. This is the genius of t-SNE over older SNE.

t-SNE Caveats

• Perplexity matters: Try 5-50. Different values can give very different plots.

• Cluster sizes are meaningless: t-SNE distorts distances. A big cluster isn't necessarily more spread out.

• Distances between clusters are meaningless: Only local structure is preserved.

• Non-deterministic: Different random seeds give different layouts.

• Slow for large data: O(n²) — use PCA first to reduce to 50 dims, then t-SNE.

• Only for visualization: Not suitable as preprocessing for ML models.

8. UMAP — The Best of Both Worlds

Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection is the modern alternative to t-SNE. It's faster, preserves more global structure, and can be used for both visualization AND as preprocessing for ML.

Advantages over t-SNE

• Much faster (minutes vs hours on large datasets)

• Preserves global structure better (cluster distances are more meaningful)

• Can embed into any number of dimensions (not just 2)

• Supports supervised and semi-supervised modes

• More stable across runs with the same parameters

How It Works (Simplified)

1. Build a weighted k-nearest-neighbor graph in high-D

2. Construct a fuzzy topological representation (simplicial complex)

3. Optimize a low-D layout to match this topology

4. Uses cross-entropy loss (vs KL divergence in t-SNE)

Key Parameters

• n_neighbors (default 15): Controls local vs global focus. Small = fine local structure. Large = preserves more global structure.

• min_dist (default 0.1): How tightly points can cluster. Small = denser clusters. Large = more spread out.

• n_components (default 2): Output dimensions. Can be >2 for ML preprocessing.

11. Other Methods

LDA (Linear Discriminant Analysis)

Supervised dimensionality reduction. Finds axes that maximize class separation (not just variance). Great when you have labels and want features that help classification.

Autoencoders

Neural networks that learn to compress data into a bottleneck layer, then reconstruct it. Can capture complex nonlinear structure. The bottleneck = reduced representation.

Random Projection

Surprisingly, projecting onto random directions preserves distances well (Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma). Extremely fast. Good when you just need "approximately lower" dimensions.

Kernel PCA

PCA with the kernel trick — handles nonlinear structure by implicitly mapping to higher dimensions first, then doing PCA there. RBF kernel is most common.

The Standard Pipeline

Step 1: PCA to reduce from thousands of features to 50-100 (fast, removes noise).
Step 2: UMAP or t-SNE on the PCA output for visualization (avoids O(n²) on raw data).
This is the standard workflow used at companies like Google, Spotify, and OpenAI for visualizing embeddings.

The Big Picture

Dimensionality reduction is about finding the "true shape" of your data hidden in a cloud of noise and redundancy. PCA finds the biggest linear axes, t-SNE and UMAP uncover nonlinear clusters and manifolds. Together they form the essential toolkit for any data scientist working with high-dimensional data — from genomics to NLP embeddings to computer vision.
