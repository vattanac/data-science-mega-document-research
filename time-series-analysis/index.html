Time Series Analysis

Understanding temporal data — stationarity, decomposition, forecasting & beyond

Statistics & Probability ARIMA Forecasting Autocorrelation Seasonality

1. What Is a Time Series?

A time series is a sequence of data points indexed (or listed or graphed) in time order. Unlike cross-sectional data, the ordering matters — each observation depends on past values.

Examples everywhere

  • Stock prices recorded every minute
  • Monthly rainfall in a city over decades
  • Server CPU usage sampled every 5 seconds
  • Weekly retail sales figures

The fundamental question in time series analysis: how can we use the past to understand — and predict — the future?

2. Components of a Time Series

Most time series can be decomposed into four components:

Trend (T)

The long-term increase or decrease. A stock index growing over years, or global temperatures rising over decades.

Seasonality (S)

Regular, repeating patterns at fixed periods — e.g., ice-cream sales peaking every summer, or web traffic dipping on weekends.

Cyclical (C)

Fluctuations at non-fixed periods, often driven by economic cycles. Unlike seasonality, the period length varies.

Residual / Noise (ε)

The random, unpredictable component left after removing trend, seasonality, and cycles.

Two decomposition models:

Additive: Y(t) = T(t) + S(t) + C(t) + ε(t)
Multiplicative: Y(t) = T(t) × S(t) × C(t) × ε(t)

4. Stationarity

A time series is stationary if its statistical properties — mean, variance, autocorrelation — do not change over time. Most forecasting models (ARIMA, etc.) assume stationarity.

Strict vs Weak Stationarity

  • Strict stationarity: The full joint distribution of any collection of time points is invariant to time shifts. Very strong condition.
  • Weak (wide-sense) stationarity: Mean is constant, variance is finite and constant, and the autocovariance between two points depends only on the lag, not on time. This is what we usually mean in practice.

Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) Test

The ADF test checks for a unit root — if one exists, the series is non-stationary. A low p-value (typically < 0.05) means we reject the null hypothesis of a unit root, concluding the series is stationary.

ΔY(t) = α + βt + γY(t-1) + Σ δᵢΔY(t-i) + ε(t) H₀: γ = 0 (unit root, non-stationary) H₁: γ < 0 (stationary)

5. Differencing & Transformations

When a series isn't stationary, we can transform it:

  • First differencing: Y'(t) = Y(t) − Y(t−1). Removes a linear trend.
  • Second differencing: Y''(t) = Y'(t) − Y'(t−1). Removes quadratic trends.
  • Seasonal differencing: Y*(t) = Y(t) − Y(t−m), where m is the seasonal period.
  • Log transform: Stabilises variance when it grows with the level.
  • Box-Cox transform: A generalised power transform parametrised by λ.
Box-Cox: Y(λ) = (Y^λ − 1) / λ when λ ≠ 0 = ln(Y) when λ = 0

6. Autocorrelation & Partial Autocorrelation

Autocorrelation measures how a series correlates with lagged copies of itself.

ACF — Autocorrelation Function

ρ(k) = Cov(Y(t), Y(t−k)) / Var(Y(t))

Plots of ACF reveal repeating patterns and help identify the order of MA models.

PACF — Partial Autocorrelation Function

The PACF at lag k is the correlation between Y(t) and Y(t−k) after removing the effect of lags 1 through k−1. It helps identify the order of AR models.

AR(p)Tails off graduallyCuts off after lag p
MA(q)Cuts off after lag qTails off gradually

8. Autoregressive (AR) Models

An AR(p) model predicts the current value as a linear combination of the past p values:

Y(t) = c + φ₁Y(t−1) + φ₂Y(t−2) + … + φₚY(t−p) + ε(t)

The series is stationary when all roots of the characteristic polynomial lie outside the unit circle (equivalently |φ₁| < 1 for AR(1)).

Intuition

Think of AR as a system with memory. An AR(1) with φ = 0.9 "remembers" heavily — shocks decay slowly. With φ = 0.2, memory is short and the series looks nearly random.

9. Moving Average (MA) Models

An MA(q) model expresses the current value as a linear combination of past error terms:

Y(t) = μ + ε(t) + θ₁ε(t−1) + θ₂ε(t−2) + … + θ_qε(t−q)

MA models are always stationary (since they're finite sums of white noise). The ACF cuts off after lag q — this is the key diagnostic signature.

10. ARIMA — Putting It Together

ARIMA(p, d, q) combines autoregression, differencing, and moving average:

  • p — order of the AR part (use PACF to choose)
  • d — number of differences needed for stationarity
  • q — order of the MA part (use ACF to choose)
ARIMA(p,d,q): (1 − Σφᵢ Bⁱ)(1 − B)ᵈ Y(t) = c + (1 + Σθⱼ Bʲ) ε(t) where B is the backshift operator: B Y(t) = Y(t−1)

Seasonal ARIMA — SARIMA

SARIMA(p,d,q)(P,D,Q)ₘ adds seasonal AR, differencing, and MA at seasonal lag m:

SARIMA: (1−ΣΦᵢBᵐⁱ)(1−Σφⱼ Bʲ)(1−Bᵐ)ᴰ(1−B)ᵈ Y(t) = (1+ΣΘᵢBᵐⁱ)(1+Σθⱼ Bʲ) ε(t)

11. Model Selection — AIC, BIC & Cross-Validation

Choosing the right (p, d, q) is critical. Common criteria:

CriterionFormulaTrade-off
AIC−2 ln(L) + 2kBalances fit vs complexity; can overfit slightly
BIC−2 ln(L) + k ln(n)Penalises complexity more; favours simpler models
AICcAIC + 2k(k+1)/(n−k−1)Corrected AIC for small samples

Lower is better for all three. In practice, try a grid of (p,d,q) values and pick the model with the lowest AIC/BIC.

Time-Series Cross-Validation

Standard k-fold CV shuffles data — bad for time series! Instead, use expanding window (train on 1…t, test on t+1) or sliding window (train on t−w…t, test on t+1).

13. Beyond ARIMA

Exponential Smoothing (ETS)

Assigns exponentially decreasing weights to past observations. Variants: Simple (SES), Holt's linear trend, Holt-Winters (trend + seasonality).

Prophet

Facebook's additive regression model: y(t) = g(t) + s(t) + h(t) + ε(t), where g is trend (linear or logistic growth), s is Fourier-based seasonality, and h captures holidays/events.

LSTM & Deep Learning

Recurrent neural networks — especially LSTMs and GRUs — can capture complex nonlinear temporal dependencies. Transformer-based models (Temporal Fusion Transformer, PatchTST) are the current frontier.

VAR — Vector Autoregression

Extends AR to multivariate time series — each variable is modelled as a linear function of its own lags and the lags of all other variables.
