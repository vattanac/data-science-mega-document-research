1. Bayesian vs Frequentist Thinking

The two major schools of statistical inference differ fundamentally in how they interpret probability:

FrequentistBayesian
Probability meansLong-run frequency of eventsDegree of belief / uncertainty
Parameters areFixed but unknown constantsRandom variables with distributions
Prior knowledgeNot formally incorporatedEncoded in prior distribution
ResultPoint estimate + confidence intervalFull posterior distribution
Small samplesUnreliable (relies on asymptotics)Works well (prior helps regularize)
Frequentist: "If I repeated this experiment infinitely, the parameter would be in this interval 95% of the time." Bayesian: "Given what I've seen, there's a 95% probability the parameter is in this interval." The Bayesian interpretation is what most people actually want.

2. Bayes' Theorem

The foundation of all Bayesian inference — a simple rule for updating beliefs with evidence:

P(θ | data) = P(data | θ) · P(θ) / P(data)

The Four Components

  • P(θ | data) — Posterior: what we believe about θ after seeing the data. This is what we want.
  • P(data | θ) — Likelihood: how probable the observed data is under parameter θ. From our statistical model.
  • P(θ) — Prior: what we believed about θ before seeing data. Encodes domain knowledge.
  • P(data) — Evidence (marginal likelihood): normalizing constant. Often ignored (just ensures posterior sums to 1).
Posterior ∝ Likelihood × Prior

This is the key formula. The posterior is proportional to the likelihood times the prior. More data → likelihood dominates → prior matters less.

3. Prior, Likelihood & Posterior

Choosing a Prior

  • Informative prior: encodes specific domain knowledge. e.g., "I know this coin is roughly fair: Beta(50,50)."
  • Weakly informative: gentle regularization. e.g., "The parameter is probably between 0 and 1: Beta(2,2)."
  • Non-informative (flat): "I have no idea": Beta(1,1) = Uniform(0,1). Lets data speak entirely.
  • Jeffreys prior: invariant to reparameterization. For Bernoulli: Beta(0.5, 0.5).

How the Posterior Evolves

With little data, the posterior looks like the prior. With lots of data, the posterior concentrates around the MLE (maximum likelihood estimate). The prior acts as regularization — preventing overfitting to small samples.

As n → ∞, the Bayesian posterior converges to the frequentist MLE regardless of the prior (as long as the prior puts non-zero density on the true value). The prior only matters for small samples.

4. Conjugate Priors

A prior is conjugate to a likelihood if the posterior has the same distributional family as the prior. This gives closed-form posterior updates — no numerical integration needed.

5. Beta-Binomial Model

The most intuitive Bayesian model. You observe k successes in n trials and want to estimate the success probability θ.

Prior: θ ~ Beta(α, β) Likelihood: k ~ Binomial(n, θ)
Posterior: θ | data ~ Beta(α + k, β + n − k)

The posterior is simply: add successes to α, add failures to β. The prior acts like "pseudo-observations."

  • Prior mean: α / (α + β)
  • Posterior mean: (α + k) / (α + β + n) — a weighted average of prior mean and sample proportion k/n
  • Posterior mode (MAP): (α + k − 1) / (α + β + n − 2)
  • Credible interval: find [a,b] such that P(a ≤ θ ≤ b | data) = 0.95
Think of α and β as "imaginary" previous observations. Beta(10,10) means "I've already seen 10 heads and 10 tails in my mind." Real data gets added on top. With enough real data, the imaginary observations become negligible.

6. Normal-Normal Model

Estimating a mean μ when observations are normally distributed with known variance σ²:

Prior: μ ~ N(μ₀, σ₀²) Likelihood: x̄ ~ N(μ, σ²/n)
Posterior: μ | data ~ N(μ_n, σ_n²)
μ_n = (μ₀/σ₀² + nx̄/σ²) / (1/σ₀² + n/σ²) σ_n² = 1 / (1/σ₀² + n/σ²)

The posterior mean is a precision-weighted average of prior mean and sample mean. Precision = 1/variance. More precise source gets more weight.

7. MAP vs Full Bayesian

  • MAP (Maximum A Posteriori): find the single most probable parameter value — the mode of the posterior. Fast, gives a point estimate, but loses uncertainty information. MAP with Gaussian prior = L2 regularization (Ridge). MAP with Laplace prior = L1 regularization (Lasso).
  • Full Bayesian: use the entire posterior distribution. Gives predictions that account for parameter uncertainty. More principled but computationally harder.
Regularization in machine learning (Ridge, Lasso) is secretly Bayesian! The regularization parameter corresponds to a prior, and the regularized solution is the MAP estimate.

8. MCMC Sampling

For complex models, the posterior can't be computed in closed form. Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) draws samples from the posterior distribution numerically.

Metropolis-Hastings Algorithm

  1. Start at a random parameter value θ₀.
  2. Propose: generate candidate θ* from a proposal distribution (e.g., Gaussian centered on current θ).
  3. Accept/Reject: compute acceptance ratio r = P(θ*|data) / P(θ|data). If r ≥ 1, accept. If r < 1, accept with probability r.
  4. Repeat. After enough iterations (burn-in), the chain of samples approximates the posterior.

Practical MCMC Tips

  • Burn-in: discard the first 1000+ samples (chain hasn't converged yet).
  • Thinning: keep every k-th sample to reduce autocorrelation.
  • Multiple chains: run 3–4 chains from different starting points to check convergence.
  • Diagnostics: use R-hat (Gelman-Rubin) < 1.01 and effective sample size > 400.
  • Modern tools: Stan, PyMC, NumPyro use advanced samplers (NUTS/HMC) that are much more efficient than basic Metropolis.

9. Bayesian Model Comparison

Compare models using the marginal likelihood (evidence) — P(data | model).

Bayes Factor = P(data | M₁) / P(data | M₂)
  • BF > 10: strong evidence for M₁.
  • BF 3–10: moderate evidence.
  • BF 1–3: weak evidence.
  • BF < 1: evidence favors M₂.

The marginal likelihood naturally penalizes model complexity (Occam's razor) — simpler models that fit the data are preferred.

10. Applications in Data Science

  • A/B Testing: compute P(variant B is better than A | data) directly. No p-value confusion.
  • Recommendation Systems: Bayesian approaches handle cold-start (new users/items with little data) gracefully via priors.
  • NLP: Naive Bayes classifier, latent Dirichlet allocation (LDA) for topic modeling.
  • Time Series: Bayesian structural time series, Kalman filters.
  • Neural Networks: Bayesian neural networks quantify prediction uncertainty.
  • Hyperparameter Tuning: Bayesian optimization (Gaussian processes) is more efficient than grid search.

14. Summary & Key Takeaways

  1. Bayes' theorem: Posterior ∝ Likelihood × Prior. Update beliefs with evidence.
  2. The prior encodes domain knowledge and regularizes small-sample estimates.
  3. Conjugate priors (Beta-Binomial, Normal-Normal) give closed-form posteriors.
  4. With enough data, the posterior converges to the MLE — the prior washes out.
  5. MAP estimation is equivalent to regularized ML (Ridge = Gaussian prior, Lasso = Laplace prior).
  6. MCMC (Metropolis-Hastings, HMC/NUTS) samples from complex posteriors numerically.
  7. Bayesian inference gives you uncertainty quantification for free — credible intervals, prediction distributions.
  8. Use tools like Stan, PyMC, NumPyro for practical Bayesian modeling.
