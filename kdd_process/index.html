Step 01 of 06
Data Selection
Choose the right data from your sources that is relevant to the problem you want to solve.
What is Data Selection?

Before mining anything, we must decide what data to use. Not all data in your database is relevant. Data selection means identifying and extracting the subset of data that relates to the analysis task.

Think of it like a chef choosing ingredients — you don't pour the entire pantry into the pot. You pick only what the recipe needs.

Our Example Scenario

🏪 Supermarket Chain — We want to understand customer buying behavior to increase sales. We select transaction data from the last 12 months.

  • Source: POS (Point of Sale) system database
  • Time Range: Jan 2023 — Dec 2023
  • Focus: Customer ID, products purchased, price, date
  • Excluded: Staff records, supplier data (not relevant)

Step 02 of 06
Data Preprocessing
Clean the raw data by handling missing values, removing noise, and fixing inconsistencies.
Why Preprocessing?

Real-world data is messy. It contains missing values, duplicate records, typos, outliers, and inconsistent formats. Mining dirty data gives wrong results — "Garbage In, Garbage Out." Preprocessing fixes this before mining begins.

1. Handle Missing Values
  • Remove rows with too many nulls
  • Fill numeric nulls with mean/median
  • Fill categorical nulls with mode
  • Use prediction to impute values
Qty = NULL → fill with median(Qty) = 1
Price = "???" → fill with mean(Price)
2. Remove Duplicates
  • Detect duplicate transaction IDs
  • Compare all columns for exact copies
  • Keep one, remove rest
Before: 2,400,000 rows

3. Remove Outliers
  • Price = $0 (free?) — suspicious
  • Qty = 9999 — likely data entry error
  • Use IQR or Z-score to detect

Step 03 of 06
Data Transformation
Convert and reshape clean data into formats suitable for mining algorithms.
What is Transformation?

Even clean data might not be in the right shape for algorithms. Transformation converts data into a format that mining algorithms can process efficiently — like normalizing numbers, encoding categories, or grouping transactions.

Normalization

Scale numeric values to a common range so one feature doesn't dominate others.

# Min-Max Normalization # Scale Price to [0, 1] Price_norm = (Price - Price_min) / (Price_max - Price_min) # Example: # Price=1.50, min=0.50, max=250 Price_norm = (1.50 - 0.50) / (250 - 0.50) = 0.004
Category Encoding

Convert text categories into numbers for algorithms.

Step 04 of 06
Data Mining
Apply algorithms to discover patterns, rules, clusters, or predictions hidden in the data.
This is the Heart of KDD

Data mining is where algorithms do the heavy lifting — finding patterns you couldn't see manually. We choose the technique based on the goal: classify, cluster, find rules, predict?

Technique 1: Association Rules

"Which products are bought together?" — Used for market basket analysis.


Key metrics:

  • Support — how often the items appear together
  • Confidence — if A bought, how likely to buy B
  • Lift — how much better than random chance

Step 05 of 06
Interpretation & Evaluation
Make sense of the patterns found. Not every pattern is useful or correct — we evaluate and filter.
Why Evaluate?

Data mining may find hundreds of patterns. Many are trivial, coincidental, or already obvious. Interpretation filters patterns that are novel, useful, and actionable. We also evaluate model accuracy and whether the pattern makes business sense.

Step 06 of 06
Knowledge Use
Apply the discovered knowledge to make real decisions and actions that create value.
The Final Goal

All previous steps lead here. The discovered knowledge must be deployed — embedded into business processes, dashboards, recommendation systems, or strategic decisions. This is where data mining creates real-world value.
