1. Paper Overview

Title: Decentralized Multi-Robot Formation Control with Onboard Relative Localization

Lead Author: Martin Saska

Group: Multi-Robot Systems Lab, Czech Technical University in Prague (CTU)

Key Publications: Multiple papers (2017–2022) in IEEE RA-L, ICRA, IROS

Platform: Custom micro aerial vehicles (MAVs) with UV-LED markers

Martin Saska's group at CTU Prague has produced one of the most comprehensive bodies of work on GPS-denied, infrastructure-free multi-UAV formation flight. Their approach enables drones to fly in precise formations using only onboard cameras and UV-LED markers on neighboring drones — no GPS, no motion capture, no ground station coordination.

Core Contribution: A complete decentralized pipeline from onboard relative localization to formation control, validated in real outdoor experiments with up to 10 UAVs flying autonomously without any external positioning infrastructure.

2. Why GPS-Free Formation Matters

Most multi-drone demonstrations rely on GPS (outdoor) or motion-capture systems (indoor). Both have critical limitations:

InfrastructureProblem
GPS~2-5m accuracy (insufficient for tight formations); denied in urban canyons, indoors, under foliage, and in adversarial environments (jamming/spoofing)
Motion CaptureRequires expensive external cameras; limited to small indoor volumes; not deployable in the field
UWB BeaconsRequires pre-installed anchor nodes; limited range; not scalable to unknown environments

For real-world applications — search and rescue, forest monitoring, military operations, cave exploration — drones must localize relative to each other using only what they carry onboard. This is the problem Saska's group solves.

IMUOnboard accelerometer + gyroscope for attitude estimation
Flight ControllerPixhawk running PX4 firmware
CommunicationWiFi mesh (low bandwidth; only for high-level coordination)

Software Pipeline

  1. Detection: UV camera detects LED markers on neighboring drones
  2. Identification: Unique blinking patterns identify which neighbor is which
  3. Relative Pose: Image position + known marker size → bearing + distance to neighbor
  4. State Estimation: Kalman filter fuses relative measurements with IMU data
  5. Formation Control: Decentralized controller computes velocity commands
  6. Low-Level Control: Pixhawk PX4 executes velocity commands

4. Onboard Relative Localization

The localization system is the paper's core technical contribution. Each drone estimates the relative position of its neighbors using only its onboard camera.

UV-LED Detection

Each drone carries UV-LEDs that blink in a unique pattern. A UV-pass filter on the camera blocks ambient light, making the LEDs appear as bright dots on a dark background — trivial to detect even in bright sunlight.

Detection: threshold(UV_image) → blob centroids → (u, v) pixel coordinates

Range Estimation

Given the known physical size of the LED marker and its apparent size in the image, the range is estimated by:

d = (f × L) / p

where f = focal length, L = physical LED spacing, p = pixel spacing in the image. Combined with the bearing from the pixel position, this gives a 3D relative position estimate at 30+ Hz.

State Estimation

A multi-target Kalman filter tracks each neighbor, fusing camera detections with IMU predictions to maintain smooth, low-latency relative position estimates even during temporary occlusions.

5. Formation Control Law

The decentralized formation controller drives each drone toward its desired position relative to its neighbors.

Virtual Structure Approach

The formation is defined as a set of desired relative positions (offsets) between drones. Each drone computes its velocity command based on the error between its current relative positions and the desired formation:

u_i = K_p × Σⱼ (p_ij - p_ij*) + K_d × Σⱼ (v_ij - v_ij*)

where p_ij is the measured relative position of neighbor j, p_ij* is the desired offset, and K_p, K_d are proportional and derivative gains.

Formation Types

Rigid Formations

  • Line, V-shape, triangle, diamond
  • Fixed inter-drone distances
  • Entire formation translates and rotates as a unit

Flexible Formations

  • Scale, stretch, deform on command
  • Adapt to obstacles or narrow passages
  • Smooth transitions between shapes

7. Consensus-Based Coordination

For the swarm to move as a unit, drones must agree on a common reference frame and velocity. This is achieved through a consensus protocol:

// Each drone runs this at every control step:
v_consensus = w_self * v_own_estimate
for each neighbor j:
    v_consensus += w_j * v_j_estimate
v_consensus /= sum(weights)

// Formation velocity = consensus + formation correction
v_command = v_consensus + K_formation * formation_error

The weights w_j can be uniform (simple average) or distance-based (closer neighbors have more influence). The consensus converges exponentially fast on connected communication graphs.

Leader-Follower Mode

Alternatively, one drone can be designated as a leader. The leader follows a mission waypoint plan while followers maintain their formation offsets relative to the leader. This is simpler but creates a single point of failure.

8. Collision Avoidance

With drones flying in close proximity (1-3m apart), collision avoidance is safety-critical.

Reactive Avoidance Layer

A repulsive potential field is added to the formation controller. When two drones get closer than a safety distance, a repulsive force pushes them apart:

F_repulsive = K_rep × (1/d - 1/d_safe)² × n̂_ij when d < d_safe

This operates independently of the formation controller and takes priority — safety always overrides formation accuracy.

10. Real-World Outdoor Experiments

Saska's group conducted extensive outdoor flight tests, which is what sets this work apart from simulation-only studies.

Key Experimental Results

  • Up to 10 UAVs flying in formation simultaneously outdoors
  • No GPS: Entire flight using only onboard UV-LED relative localization
  • Formation accuracy: ±0.3m position error at 2m inter-drone spacing
  • Wind tolerance: Stable in winds up to 5 m/s (light breeze)
  • Formation transitions: Smooth switching between V, line, diamond, and circle formations in flight
  • Longest flight: ~8 minutes continuous formation flight (battery-limited)
  • Localization rate: 30 Hz onboard, using ~15% of CPU on Raspberry Pi 4

12. Applications

Search & Rescue

Coordinated UAV teams that sweep areas in formation, maintaining overlapping camera coverage. GPS-free operation is essential in collapsed structures and urban canyons.

Forest Monitoring

Multi-drone formations flying under canopy where GPS is denied. UV-LED localization works even in dappled sunlight conditions with the UV-pass filter.

Infrastructure Inspection

Coordinated inspection of bridges, power lines, and wind turbines. Formation flying ensures systematic coverage with minimal redundancy and no missed areas.

Military & Defense

GPS-denied environments are common in contested airspace. Self-localizing drone swarms that cannot be jammed or spoofed have clear tactical advantages.

14. Key Takeaways

  1. GPS is a crutch: Real-world deployments often can't rely on GPS. Onboard relative localization is the path to truly autonomous drone swarms.
  2. UV-LED markers provide a simple, low-cost, and robust solution for inter-drone localization that works outdoors in sunlight.
  3. Decentralized control eliminates single points of failure and scales naturally — each drone runs the same algorithm.
  4. Formation accuracy of ±0.3m at 30 Hz is sufficient for tight formation flying and far exceeds GPS-based approaches.
  5. Safety-first design: The repulsive collision avoidance layer takes priority over formation accuracy — better to temporarily break formation than to collide.
  6. Real outdoor validation with 10 UAVs distinguishes this work from the many simulation-only studies in multi-UAV research.
  7. Modular architecture: Detection → Identification → Estimation → Control pipeline allows each component to be upgraded independently.
Impact: Saska's group at CTU Prague has established one of the leading programs in GPS-denied multi-UAV flight, with their open-source MRS UAV System used by research groups worldwide. Their work bridges the gap between theoretical swarm control and reliable field deployment.
