Part 4: Hardware & Electronics

Every component that makes a drone fly.

From motors and sensors to compute platforms and communication hardware, this section breaks down the physical systems that enable swarm drones to perceive their environment, compute decisions, and execute synchronized flight.

Section 1: Drone Anatomy — Every Part Explained

Why this matters for swarms: Every drone in your swarm must be mechanically identical to ensure predictable thrust, weight, and handling characteristics. A single mismatched motor or propeller creates inconsistency that cascades through formation control and synchronized maneuvers.

1.1 Frame

The frame is the skeletal structure that holds everything together. It determines the drone's weight, size, and mechanical stiffness.

Materials

Material Strength/Weight Cost Durability Swarm Use
Carbon Fiber Excellent $$$ High (brittle) Professional swarms
Aluminum Good $$ Medium (bends) Budget swarms
3D Printed (PETG) Fair $ Low (flexible) Custom prototypes

Configurations

  • Quadcopter (4 motors): Most common, simple control, no redundancy. Best for swarms where drones are expendable.
  • Hexacopter (6 motors): Can lose one motor and still fly. More weight, more power consumption.
  • Octocopter (8 motors): Heavy lift, extreme redundancy. Expensive, overkill for most swarms.

Size Classes

Class Wheelbase Weight Example
Nano <100mm 5-20g Crazyflie 2.1
Micro 100-250mm 100-250g DJI Mini series
Small 250-500mm 500g-1.5kg FPV Racing drones
Medium 500mm+ 1.5kg+ Delivery drones

Thrust-to-Weight Ratio

Critical for swarm performance:

  • ≥2:1 = agile, responsive (racing, acrobatics)
  • 1-2:1 = standard flight (formation, mapping)
  • <1:1 = cannot hover (disaster)

For synchronized swarms, target 2-3:1 to ensure all drones have identical acceleration and responsiveness.

Quadcopter Frame Layout (Top View): Motor 1 (Front-Left, CCW) ↓ ┌─────────┐ │ │ Wheelbase (W) │ FC │ │ │ └─────────┘ ↑ ↑ Motor 4 Motor 2 (Rear-Left) (Front-Right) (CW) (CCW) Motor 3 (Rear-Right, CW) ↓ Side View: Motor arms typically 5-15° down for stability
Pro Tip: Weight Budget

Build a weight breakdown for your frame design. In swarms, each drone should be ±10g of the target weight. Heavier drones consume more battery and fly slower; lighter ones may be underpowered. Use a precision scale during assembly.

1.2 Motors

Brushless DC (BLDC) motors are the standard for drones. They're efficient, durable, and controllable.

Key Specifications

Spec Definition Impact
KV Rating RPM per volt Higher KV = faster, less torque. Lower KV = slower, more torque.
Stator Size 2205 = 22mm diameter, 5mm height Larger stator = more torque. Smaller = lighter, faster.
Max Current (A) Continuous & burst limits Must not exceed ESC current rating.
Weight Grams Larger motors = heavier. 4× motors per drone!

KV Selection Guide

  • Low KV (450-900): Large props, heavy drones, slow but efficient. Endurance flights.
  • Medium KV (1200-2300): Balanced. Most swarms use 1500-2000 KV.
  • High KV (2500+): Small props, light drones, fast but power-hungry. Racing.
Swarm Application

For a 10-drone swarm, buy 5 spare motors (50% spare). Motors fail from stress/bearing wear. Stock the same KV/size for all drones—don't mix motor types in your swarm.

1.3 Propellers

Propellers convert motor rotation into thrust. Mismatched or damaged props are the #1 cause of swarm instability.

Propeller Specs

Parameter Definition Example: 10×4.5
Diameter Inches (side-to-side) 10 inches = 25.4cm
Pitch Distance traveled per revolution 4.5 inches per rotation
Material Plastic, carbon fiber, wood Plastic for training, CF for efficiency
Rotation CW or CCW Must pair correctly to cancel torque

Thrust vs. Diameter & Pitch

  • Larger diameter: More thrust at lower RPM. More efficient, less noise.
  • Higher pitch: More thrust at higher RPM. Faster but power-hungry.
Propeller Orientation (Top View): Prop 1 (CCW) Prop 2 (CW) ↺ ↻ ┌─────────────────────────┐ │ │ │ Creates lift │ Torque cancels │ Upward thrust │ │ │ └─────────────────────────┘ ↻ ↺ Prop 4 (CW) Prop 3 (CCW)
Pro Tip: Propeller Inspection

Before every flight, inspect all propellers for cracks, warping, or dents. A damaged prop creates vibration that destabilizes the entire swarm. Keep 20+ spare props per drone—they break constantly during training.

1.4 Electronic Speed Controller (ESC)

The ESC converts the flight controller's PWM signal into 3-phase AC current to drive the BLDC motor. Without an ESC, motors can't receive commands.

ESC Specifications

Spec What It Means Importance
Current Rating (A) Max continuous & burst current Critical: Must exceed motor peak current by 20%
Protocol PWM, OneShot, DShot (digital) DShot (600/1200) = no calibration, faster response
LiPo Cell Count 3S (11.1V), 4S (14.8V), 6S (22.2V) Must match your battery voltage
BLHeli Firmware Open-source ESC firmware Allows tuning, debugging, & updates via USB

4-in-1 vs. Individual ESCs

4-in-1 ESC (Integrated)

  • All 4 ESCs on one board
  • Saves weight: 1 board instead of 4
  • Single point of failure: broken board = unflying drone
  • Best for: small swarm drones, space-constrained designs

Individual ESCs

  • 4 separate ESCs mounted on frame arms
  • One failed ESC = one motor down (hexacopter can survive)
  • Harder to wire, heavier total weight
  • Best for: larger drones, redundancy-critical swarms
Real-World Analogy

Think of the ESC as a dimmer switch. The flight controller says "spin to 30% power," and the ESC modulates the voltage/frequency to achieve exactly that rotation speed. No ESC = motor gets raw battery voltage and spins uncontrollably.

1.5 Battery (LiPo)

Lithium-Polymer batteries dominate drone propulsion. They're high energy density but require careful handling to prevent fire.

LiPo Naming Convention

Example: 4S 1500mAh 100C LiPo

Parameter Meaning Example
Cell Count (S) Nominal voltage = S × 3.7V 4S = 14.8V nominal, 16.8V full, 12V depleted
Capacity (mAh) Energy storage (milliamp-hours) 1500mAh = 1.5Ah. 5000mAh = endurance
C-Rating Discharge multiplier 100C × 1500mAh = 150A max burst
Max Discharge C × mAh (amps) 1500mAh 100C = max 150A draw

Flight Time Estimation

Formula: Flight Time (min) = Capacity (mAh) / Average Current (mA) × 60

Example: 1500mAh battery, average draw 20A (20,000 mA) → 1500 / 20,000 × 60 = 4.5 minutes

Voltage Discharge Curve (4S LiPo): Voltage (V) 16.8V ├─ Fully charged (dangerous zone!) │ 14.8V ├────●────●────●────● │ ╲ ╲ ╲ ╲ Good flight region 13.0V ├─────●────●────●────●──○ │ ╲ ╲ ╲ ╱ 12.0V ├──────────────●──●────● Alarm threshold │ ╲ ╱ 11.1V ├──────────────────● Minimum safe (3.0V/cell) │ └─ Time / Capacity Consumed (%) Never discharge below 3.0V per cell!

Swarm Battery Strategy

  • Identical batteries: All drones in the swarm should use the same capacity, voltage, and C-rating for synchronized flight times.
  • Battery rotation: Each drone carries 2 batteries. Land, swap, launch. Maximize swarm uptime.
  • Storage voltage: Store LiPos at 3.8-3.85V per cell (~60% charge). Prevents capacity loss.
  • Fire prevention: Charge in a LiPo-safe bag. Never leave batteries charging unattended.
Pro Tip: Battery Labeling

Mark each battery with a cycle count and date. Track capacity degradation with a discharge tester. When a battery drops below 85% of rated capacity, retire it. Degraded batteries become safety risks.

1.6 Power Distribution Board (PDB)

The PDB is the electrical hub. It takes battery power in and distributes it to ESCs, flight controller, and accessories.

PDB Features

Feature Purpose Required?
Power Rails Distribute unregulated battery voltage to ESCs Yes (essential)
5V Regulator Step down to 5V for flight controller & receiver Yes
12V Boost (optional) Supply 12V for LED, cameras, accessories Optional (nice-to-have)
Current Sensor Measure real-time current draw for battery monitoring Optional (useful for endurance flights)
Pro Tip: Capacitor on PDB

Many PDBs include a large capacitor (470µF–2200µF) to smooth voltage spikes when ESCs switch. This reduces voltage dips during aggressive maneuvers. Ensure the cap is rated for your battery voltage (6S = 22.2V peak).

Section 2: Sensors — The Drone's Senses

Why this matters for swarms: Sensors are how drones perceive position, orientation, velocity, altitude, and obstacles. Inconsistent sensor quality across your swarm creates position estimate drift, which breaks formation control. Every drone must have the same sensor package and calibration.

2.1 IMU (Inertial Measurement Unit)

The IMU is the core sensor for flight stabilization. It measures acceleration and angular velocity at 1-8 kHz, enabling tight feedback loops.

IMU Grades

Type Sensors Use Case Price
6-DOF 3-axis accel + 3-axis gyro Stabilization, basic flight $5-15
9-DOF + 3-axis magnetometer Heading reference, yaw hold $15-30
10-DOF + barometer Altitude hold, pressure trending $20-40

Common IMU Chips

  • MPU6050: Classic, cheap, noisy. Learning projects.
  • ICM20689: Better noise floor, 8kHz sampling. Racing drones.
  • BMI270: High-precision, low drift. Professional drones.

Key Parameters

  • Sampling Rate: 1-8 kHz. Higher = tighter control but more CPU load. Typical: 1-4 kHz.
  • Noise Density: Lower is better. Look for <100 µg/√Hz for accelerometers, <0.05°/s/√Hz for gyros.
  • Bias Drift: Angular velocity bias changes with temperature. Causes yaw drift. Mitigated by EKF (Extended Kalman Filter).
Real-World Analogy

The IMU is like your inner ear (vestibular system). It senses acceleration and rotation in real-time, letting you balance without looking. Raw IMU data is noisy (like feeling tipsy), so the flight controller filters it through an EKF to get a clean signal.

2.2 Barometer

Atmospheric pressure drops predictably with altitude. A barometer provides a vertical reference independent of GPS.

Barometer Specs

  • Accuracy: ±0.1 hPa (≈ ±1 meter altitude)
  • Resolution: 1 Pa (≈ 8cm)
  • Sample Rate: 50-100 Hz
  • Limitations: Affected by wind, temperature variations, nearby heat sources

Use in Swarms

  • Altitude hold: maintain constant height during formation flight
  • GPS-denied regions: barometer provides vertical control when GPS is unavailable
  • Vertical takeoff calibration: establish starting altitude baseline
Pro Tip: Barometer Drift

Barometers drift with temperature and weather changes. Recalibrate the pressure baseline every 30 minutes during long flights. In a swarm, sync all drones' altimeter baselines before launching formation.

2.3 GPS & RTK GPS

GPS triangulation from satellites is essential for outdoor swarms. RTK (Real-Time Kinematic) GPS provides cm-level accuracy for precise formations.

Standard GPS

  • Accuracy: 2-5 meters (civilian, standard)
  • Update Rate: 5-20 Hz
  • Acquisition Time: 30-60 seconds cold start, 5-10 seconds warm start
  • Limitations: Multipath (urban canyons), atmospheric delay, satellite geometry
  • Cost: $20-100

RTK GPS

  • Accuracy: 2cm horizontal, 3cm vertical (with base station)
  • How it works: Drone + ground-based reference station both track satellites. Compare carrier-phase measurements to eliminate atmospheric errors.
  • Requirements: Base station (fixed or local), correction radio link or internet
  • Cost: $200-600 per drone + $500-2000 base station
  • Latency: 5-50ms (critical for real-time swarm control)

GPS-Denied Alternatives

Technology Accuracy Environment Cost
Ultra-Wideband (UWB) 10cm-1m Indoor/dense urban $100-300 per drone + anchors
Motion Capture 1-5mm Indoor (lab) $50k-200k system
Visual SLAM 5-50cm Indoor/outdoor with features Embedded on drone
Swarm Application: RTK Formations

For precise outdoor swarms (>10 drones), RTK GPS enables cm-level formation stability. Each drone carries an RTK receiver. A single base station on the ground corrects all drones simultaneously. Total cost ≈ 10 × $300 + $1000 base = $4000.

2.4 Magnetometer (Compass)

Measures Earth's magnetic field to establish absolute heading/yaw reference.

Magnetometer Use

  • Yaw initialization: Figure-out drone heading at startup
  • Heading hold: Resist drift of gyroscope yaw estimate
  • EKF heading aiding: Fusion of mag + gyro for accurate heading

Problems & Solutions

Problem Cause Solution
Magnetic interference ESCs, motors, battery create local fields Mount mag away from electronics; calibrate on-site
Heading oscillation Poor calibration, low confidence Figure-8 calibration at flying location
Yaw drift Mag disabled due to interference Gyro-only yaw (acceptable for short flights)
Pro Tip: Swarm Compass Calibration

Calibrate the magnetometer at your flying location before every session. Magnetic interference varies by location. Do a slow figure-8 rotation 5 meters away from metal structures and power lines.

2.5 LiDAR (Light Detection & Ranging)

LiDAR uses laser pulses to measure distance to objects. Essential for obstacle avoidance and autonomous mapping in swarms.

LiDAR Types

Type Measurement Range Update Weight Cost
1D (TFMini) Single-point distance 0.3-12m 100 Hz 5g $40
2D (RPLidar A1) 360° scan 0.15-12m 10 Hz 170g $99
3D (Velodyne) 3D point cloud 0.5-100m 10 Hz 900g $5000

Swarm LiDAR Strategy

  • Lightweight swarms: Use 1D downward-facing LiDAR for altitude fallback & ground distance
  • Obstacle-dense environments: 2D LiDAR for fast obstacle mapping and avoidance
  • Large swarms: Centralized mapping (one drone or ground station processes LiDAR) to reduce per-drone computation
Real-World Analogy

1D LiDAR = reading a range finder (distance to wall ahead). 2D LiDAR = sonar scan (full circle). 3D LiDAR = seeing in 3D like human eyes. Higher dimension = more info but more power and weight.

2.6 Camera Systems

Cameras enable visual navigation, object detection, tracking, and mapping. The choice depends on your swarm mission.

Camera Types & Tradeoffs

Type Sensors Use Weight Cost
Monocular Single RGB camera Visual odometry, detection, 2D tracking 10-50g $20-100
Stereo Two synchronized cameras 3D reconstruction, depth estimation, obstacle detection 50-150g $100-300
RGB-D Color + depth sensor (IR projector + detector) Real-time 3D mapping, close-range object detection 100-200g $150-400
Thermal (FLIR) Infrared only Night search & rescue, heat detection 50-100g $500-2000

FOV (Field of View)

  • Standard (50-90°): Balanced for navigation & tracking
  • Wide/Fisheye (120-160°): More scene context, SLAM works better
  • Narrow (20-40°): Telephoto, good for tracking distant objects

For Swarms: Downward-Facing vs. Forward-Facing

Downward-Facing

  • Optical flow for velocity estimation (no GPS)
  • Ground texture for visual odometry
  • Works at very low altitude

Forward-Facing

  • Obstacle detection for avoidance
  • Target detection & tracking
  • Situational awareness for swarms

2.7 Ultra-Wideband (UWB)

UWB provides indoor positioning at cm-level accuracy. Essential for indoor swarms where GPS is unavailable.

UWB Positioning Basics

  • Principle: Time-of-flight (ToF) measurement from 4+ fixed anchors
  • Accuracy: 10cm-1m depending on LoS and multipath
  • Range: 50-100m outdoor, 10-50m indoor
  • Latency: 10-100ms (suitable for swarm control)

Practical Setup

  • Mount 4+ anchors (stationary, known positions) around flying space
  • Each drone carries a UWB tag receiver (DW1000/DW3000)
  • Tag broadcasts range requests; anchors respond with their location
  • Drone computes 3D position via trilateration

UWB Chip Options

Chip Frequency Power Cost
DW1000 3.5-6.5 GHz 80mW peak $40
DW3000 3.5-6.5 GHz 50mW peak $60
Crazyflie Loco DW1000-based Integrated package $50-100 per unit
Indoor Swarm Example: Crazyflie + Loco Positioning

The Crazyflie 2.1 is a popular nano-quadcopter (10cm) with integrated UWB support. Mount 8 Loco anchors around a 20m×20m room. Each Crazyflie (~50g) carries a tag, providing cm-level position estimates at 10 Hz. Perfect for 5-10 drone swarms indoors.

2.8 Optical Flow

Optical flow sensors track texture movement on the ground to estimate drone velocity—no GPS needed.

How It Works

  • Downward-facing camera (or dedicated sensor) tracks pixels
  • Image correlation: Compare consecutive frames to estimate motion
  • Velocity estimate: Pixel displacement × altitude = XY velocity
  • Requirements: Textured ground (not blank white), altitude <5m, good lighting

Optical Flow Sensors

  • PMW3901: Dedicated 35×35 pixel sensor, very cheap ($5-15), 100 Hz
  • Camera-based: Standard RGB camera with software processing, better FOV

Swarm Use: GPS-Denied Indoors

  • Pair optical flow with barometer for altitude hold
  • Use UWB for absolute position; optical flow for between-anchor velocity
  • Requires textured floor (carpet, checkerboard pattern), not polished wood or concrete
Pro Tip: Optical Flow Calibration

Optical flow estimates vary with ground texture and lighting. Calibrate in your specific flying environment. A featureless white room will cause complete failure—add patterns or use it only on textured floors.

Section 3: Compute Platforms — The Drone's Brain

Why this matters for swarms: Drones have a two-tier compute architecture: a real-time flight controller handles motor control, and a companion computer handles AI, planning, and inter-drone communication. Standardizing both tiers ensures all swarm drones behave identically.

3.1 Flight Controller (Low-Level Control)

The flight controller runs at 1-4 kHz, reading IMU/sensors and commanding motors. It's the drone's spinal cord.

Flight Controller Comparison

Controller Processor Clock Sensors Price Swarm Suit
Pixhawk 4 STM32F7 216 MHz IMU, Baro, Compass, SD $100 Excellent
Pixhawk 6C STM32H7 480 MHz Dual IMU, Baro, Compass $150 Excellent
Kakute H7 STM32H7 480 MHz Integrated ESC support $80 Good (FPV racing)
Cube Orange STM32H7 480 MHz Modular, redundant $200 Professional

Flight Controller Firmware

  • PX4: Open-source, modular, excellent for research/swarms
  • ArduPilot: Feature-rich, large community, stable
  • Betaflight: FPV racing, not ideal for precise formation control

Real-Time Control Loop

Flight Controller Main Loop (1 kHz): ┌─────────────────────────────────────┐ │ 1. Read IMU (accel + gyro) │ ← 1 ms │ 2. Sensor fusion (EKF) │ │ 3. Attitude estimation │ │ 4. Run attitude controller │ │ 5. Compute motor commands (PWM) │ │ 6. Send to ESCs │ └─────────────────────────────────────┘ ↓ (repeat) Total latency: ~2-5 ms Fast enough for manual flight, but companion computer adds 10-100 ms for perception.

3.2 Companion Computer (High-Level Compute)

A companion computer (Raspberry Pi, Jetson, etc.) runs on the drone alongside the flight controller. It handles AI, planning, and swarm coordination.

Companion Computer Options

Platform CPU/GPU Power Draw Weight Cost AI Capability
RPi 4 Model B BCM2711 (4-core ARM) 3-5W 50g $75 Basic (TensorFlow Lite)
RPi 5 BCM2712 (4-core ARM) 5-10W 55g $80 Improved (faster inference)
Jetson Nano ARM + 128 CUDA cores 5W idle, 10W load 65g $99 Good (20-40 TOPS)
Jetson Xavier NX ARM + 384 CUDA cores 10W idle, 20W load 70g $250 Excellent (20 TFLOPS)
Jetson Orin Nano ARM + 1024 CUDA cores 5W idle, 15W load 80g $200 Excellent (40 TOPS)

Power Budget

A 5000mAh 4S LiPo battery (14.8V, 74Wh) powering a drone with:

  • 4× 25A motors @ 50% throttle = 50W
  • Flight controller = 2W
  • Jetson Nano = 10W
  • Total: 62W → ~1.2 hours flight time

Jetson vs. Raspberry Pi for Swarms

Raspberry Pi 4/5

  • Cost: cheap ($75)
  • Community: huge (most tutorials)
  • GPU: no hardware acceleration
  • Best for: vision processing, ROS, basic swarm coordination

Jetson Nano / Orin Nano

  • Cost: moderate ($99-200)
  • GPU: NVIDIA CUDA (neural networks, parallel compute)
  • Best for: real-time object detection, swarm AI, autonomous decision-making

3.3 Flight Controller + Companion Architecture

The two-tier compute model separates real-time control from high-level planning.

Swarm Drone Compute Architecture: ┌────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ Companion Computer (Jetson Nano) │ │ - ROS / custom software │ │ - Object detection, tracking │ │ - Path planning, swarm coordination │ │ - Runs at 10-30 Hz │ │ │ │ Inter-drone comms (WiFi/UWB/XBee) │ │ ↓ (sends target positions @ 10Hz) │ └────────────────────────────────────────────┘ ↓ UART/MAVROS Bridge ┌────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ Flight Controller (Pixhawk / STM32H7) │ │ - PX4 / ArduPilot firmware │ │ - Sensor fusion (EKF) │ │ - Motor control loop (1 kHz) │ │ - Stabilization (no thinking needed) │ └────────────────────────────────────────────┘ ↓ PWM/DShot to ESCs ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ 4× ESCs + Motors │ └─────────────────────────────────────────────┘ Latency Budget: - Companion decision: 50-100 ms - MAVROS transmission: 2-5 ms - FC processing + motor update: 2-5 ms - Total: 54-110 ms (safe for formation control)

Communication Between FC & Companion

  • Serial (UART): 57,600-500,000 baud. Standard MAVLINK protocol. Simple, reliable.
  • MAVROS (ROS middleware): Provides publish-subscribe interface to flight controller. Ideal for swarm coordination.
  • SDK (e.g., Dronekit-Python): Python API for drone control. Good for rapid prototyping.
Real-World Analogy

The flight controller is like your spinal cord + brainstem: handles reflexes (stability, balance) at high speed. The companion computer is like your cerebral cortex: slow but clever (planning, reasoning, communication). They talk via the MAVLINK protocol, which is like your corpus callosum.

Section 4: Communication Hardware

Why this matters for swarms: Drones must communicate with ground control, each other, and stay under manual override. Reliable comms are critical for safety and swarm coordination.

4.1 Radio Control (RC)

RC receivers allow manual pilot override. Non-negotiable safety feature.

RC Transmitter/Receiver Specs

  • Frequency: 2.4 GHz (universal, license-free in most countries)
  • Channels: 4-16 channels (throttle, pitch, roll, yaw, + aux switches)
  • Range: 1-40 km depending on antenna and power
  • Protocols: SBUS, PPM, CRSF (Crossfire, newer)

Popular RC Systems for Swarms

System Protocol Range Latency Cost
Taranis X9D + X4R SBUS 1-2 km 7 ms $250
Crossfire Transmitter + Receiver CRSF 40 km 7 ms $300
FrSky Taranis QX7 SBUS/D16 1-2 km 7 ms $200

4.2 Telemetry Radios

Telemetry radios send drone data (GPS, battery, attitude) back to ground control and allow command injection.

SiK Radios (Popular for Drones)

  • Frequency: 433 MHz or 915 MHz (region-dependent)
  • Range: 1-2 km line-of-sight (can extend to 5km with external antennas)
  • Bandwidth: 57,600 baud (sufficient for MAVLINK telemetry)
  • Latency: 10-50 ms
  • Cost: $30-60 per pair (drone + ground)

Telemetry Protocol: MAVLink

  • Micro Aerial Vehicle Link: Standardized binary protocol for drone communication
  • Heartbeat: Regular drone status broadcasts (GPS, battery, attitude)
  • Command injection: Ground station can send waypoints, mode changes, etc.
  • Bandwidth efficient: ~50 bytes/sec for basic telemetry on 10 drones

Swarm Telemetry Strategy

  • Single ground telemetry: Centralized receiver for all 10 drones' data
  • Bandwidth sharing: Stagger telemetry updates (drone 1 @ 0ms, 2 @ 10ms, 3 @ 20ms...)
  • Fallback: If telemetry lost, drones continue mission (autonomous)

4.3 WiFi

WiFi provides high bandwidth for real-time video/data transfer. Limited range but useful for indoor swarms.

WiFi on Drones

  • 2.4 GHz vs. 5 GHz: 2.4 GHz better range, 5 GHz faster
  • Power draw: 0.5-2W (significant)
  • Range: 50-200m depending on environment and antenna
  • Latency: 5-50ms (variable, affected by network congestion)

WiFi Mesh Networking

  • Drones act as WiFi relays, extending range
  • Drone A connects to ground, Drone B connects to Drone A
  • Seamless but adds latency (2× per hop)
  • Good for outdoor swarms covering large areas

WiFi Module: ESP32

  • Cheap ($5): Popular for DIY swarms
  • WiFi + Bluetooth: Dual radios for flexibility
  • Power: 80-160 mA peak, can be powered from drone battery
  • Limitations: Only ~100m range solo, not reliable for precise control
WiFi Swarm Example: Indoor Mesh

10 Crazyflie drones, each with ESP32 WiFi module. Drones relay video + position data through the mesh. Ground station (laptop with WiFi adapter) monitors and commands all 10. Works within a building but adds 100+ ms latency (too high for tight formation control).

4.4 XBee / Zigbee Mesh

Zigbee is a low-power mesh networking protocol. Perfect for inter-drone communication in dense swarms.

Zigbee Specs

  • Frequency: 2.4 GHz (same as WiFi/Bluetooth, shared spectrum)
  • Range: 100m-1.5km depending on environment and antenna
  • Bandwidth: 250 kbps (low, but sufficient for swarm coordination)
  • Power: 10-100 mA (moderate)
  • Topology: Mesh (automatic routing)

XBee Pro S2C (Popular)

  • 20 dBm output power
  • 1.6km range in open space
  • Self-healing mesh (if one node dies, others reroute)
  • Cost: $20-50 per module

Swarm Mesh Coordination Example

  • Drone 1 (leader): Broadcasts target formation via Zigbee
  • Drones 2-10 (followers): Receive message, update their local position targets
  • All drones maintain formation locally using on-board sensors (no centralized control)
  • If link drops: swarms continue on last known formation (autonomous)
Pro Tip: Swarm Communication Stack

Use SiK telemetry for ground control (reliable, low-latency), and Zigbee for inter-drone coordination (mesh, autonomous failover). This separates concerns: RC/ground ↔ drone, drone ↔ drone.

Why this matters for swarms: You'll build and maintain 10+ identical drones. Professional tools save time and prevent mistakes (bad solder joints, crossed wires, damaged components).

5.2 Testing Equipment

  • Thrust Stand: Measure motor + prop efficiency. Cost $30-100. Essential if optimizing swarm designs.
  • LiPo Charger (ISDT/ToolkitRC): Balanced charging, temperature monitoring. Cost $50-150.
  • Smoke Stopper (XT60 current limiter): Protects against shorts on first power-up. Cost $10. Highly recommended!
  • LiPo Voltage Checker: Quick cell voltage check. Cost $5-20.
  • Battery Capacity Tester: Track degradation over time. Cost $20-60.

5.3 Safety Equipment

  • LiPo-Safe Charging Bag: Fire containment if battery fails. Cost $10-30. Non-negotiable!
  • Fire Extinguisher (Class C/D): For lithium fires. Cost $50-150.
  • Propeller Guards: Safety for indoor testing. Cost $20-50.
  • Safety Netting/Enclosure: Contain flying propellers. Cost $100-500.
  • Eye Protection: Props can shatter. Cost $10.
Pro Tip: Soldering Station Temperature

Set soldering iron to 350-380°C for electronics. Too hot = damage traces; too cold = cold joints. Use rosin-core solder (60/40 or lead-free). For swarm drones, pre-tin all pads before assembly to speed up building.

Section 6: Swarm-Specific Hardware Considerations

Building 10+ identical drones introduces manufacturing and logistics challenges.

6.1 Standardization & Consistency

  • Buy in bulk: Order all parts for 10+ drones at once to ensure identical batches (same manufacturing date = same reliability).
  • Quality control: Test every motor, ESC, and battery on a thrust stand or load tester before assembly.
  • Spare parts policy: For every 10 drones, buy 2-3 extra of: motors, props, ESCs, batteries. Things break!
  • Drone labeling: Label each drone (Drone 01, Drone 02...) and track parts/history in a spreadsheet.

6.2 Charging Infrastructure

Parallel Charging Board

  • Charge 4-6 batteries simultaneously from a single charger
  • Reduces turnaround time between flights
  • Cost: $30-100
  • Typical scenario: Land 10 drones, swap batteries, launch 10 fresh ones in <60 seconds

6.3 Swarm Deployment Model

  • Quick-swap battery design: Batteries accessible in <10 seconds
  • Battery rotation: Rotate 3 batteries per drone (1 flying, 2 charging)
  • Charger capacity: For 10 drones with 2 batteries each = 20 batteries. Charger must handle 4-6 in parallel. Total charge time: 3-4 hours for full rotation.
