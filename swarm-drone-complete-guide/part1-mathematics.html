Part 1: Mathematics for Swarm Drones

From vectors and calculus to probability and graph theory—learn the mathematical foundations that enable autonomous drone swarms to think, move, and coordinate. Every equation here appears in real drone firmware.

Section 1: Linear Algebra (The Language of Drones)

Linear algebra is the mathematical language drones speak. Every position, velocity, force, and transformation can be expressed as vectors and matrices. Understanding linear algebra is essential because it lets you think about drone motion in compact, precise notation.

1.1 Vectors — Position, Velocity, Force

A vector is a quantity that has both magnitude (how much) and direction (which way). Unlike a scalar (just a number), a vector tells you where something is and how it's moving.

What is a Vector?

Imagine you're navigating to a restaurant. Saying "5 km away" is useless—you need direction too. "5 km northeast" is a vector: 5 km is the magnitude, northeast is the direction. In 2D:

2D Vector Notation
v = [vx, vy]
or equivalently:
v = vxi + vyj

For a 3D drone, we add a vertical component:

3D Vector Notation
v = [vx, vy, vz]

Common Vectors in Drone Control

  • Position: p = [x, y, z] — where is the drone? (in meters, relative to origin)
  • Velocity: v = [vx, vy, vz] — how fast is it moving in each direction? (m/s)
  • Acceleration: a = [ax, ay, az] — how is speed changing? (m/s²)
  • Thrust/Force: F = [Fx, Fy, Fz] — how hard are the propellers pushing? (Newtons)

Vector Addition: Combining Forces

When multiple forces act on a drone (gravity pulling down, thrust pushing up, wind pushing sideways), we add them as vectors:

Vector Addition
F_total = F_thrust + F_gravity + F_wind
= [Tx, Ty, Tz] + [0, 0, -mg] + [Wx, Wy, 0]
= [Tx + Wx, Ty, Tz - mg]

Scalar Multiplication: Throttle Control

Scaling a vector means multiplying every component by the same number. This is how throttle works—you scale the thrust vector:

Scalar Multiplication
throttle × F_thrust = t × [Fx, Fy, Fz] = [t·Fx, t·Fy, t·Fz]
where 0 ≤ t ≤ 1 (0% to 100% throttle)

Vector Magnitude: How Fast Are You Going?

The magnitude (or length) of a velocity vector tells you the speed:

Vector Magnitude (Euclidean Norm)
||v|| = √(vx² + vy² + vz²)

Example: if v = [3, 4, 0], then ||v|| = √(9 + 16 + 0) = √25 = 5 m/s.

Unit Vectors: Direction Without Magnitude

A unit vector has magnitude 1—it points in a direction without telling you "how far." You create one by dividing a vector by its magnitude:

Unit Vector (Normalization)
û = v / ||v||

Unit vectors are useful for defining directions (north, east, up) independently of speed.

Think of a drone at your house trying to reach a restaurant 5 km northeast. The vector [5 sin(45°), 5 cos(45°)] = [3.54, 3.54] tells you the exact displacement. The unit vector [0.707, 0.707] just says "go that way" without specifying the distance.

In Real Drones: The flight controller constantly reads the current position and velocity vectors from sensors (GPS, IMU). It compares them to the desired position/velocity vectors (set by the user or path planner) and computes an error vector. It then scales this error by control gains to produce thrust commands.

1.2 Dot Product — Alignment Detection

The dot product is a way to multiply two vectors that gives you a single number (scalar). It measures how much two vectors point in the same direction.

Dot Product (Algebraic Definition)
a · b = ax·bx + ay·by + az·bz

There's another way to compute it using geometry:

Dot Product (Geometric Definition)
a · b = ||a|| · ||b|| · cos(θ)

where θ is the angle between the vectors.

Interpreting the Dot Product

  • Positive: vectors point in roughly the same direction (θ < 90°)
  • Zero: vectors are perpendicular (θ = 90°)
  • Negative: vectors point in opposite directions (θ > 90°)

Imagine two people walking. The dot product of their velocity vectors tells you if they're going the same way. If you're both walking north at 1 m/s, your velocities are aligned (high positive dot product). If one walks north and one walks east, they're perpendicular (dot product = 0). If one walks north and one south, they're opposite (negative dot product).

In Drone Swarms: Alignment is a core rule in swarm behavior. Each drone wants to match the velocity direction of its neighbors. The dot product lets the swarm easily check: "Is my neighbor flying the same direction as me?" If v_me · v_neighbor is large and positive, yes. If it's negative or zero, they're flying apart, so adjust.

1.3 Cross Product — Rotation and Torque

The cross product multiplies two vectors to produce a new vector perpendicular to both. It's used to compute torque and rotational forces.

Cross Product (3D)
a × b =
[ay·bz − az·by, az·bx − ax·bz, ax·by − ay·bx]

The Right-Hand Rule

To find the direction of a × b: point your right hand's fingers in the direction of a, curl them toward b. Your thumb points in the direction of a × b.

Magnitude of Cross Product

Cross Product Magnitude
||a × b|| = ||a|| · ||b|| · sin(θ)

The magnitude is largest when the vectors are perpendicular (sin(90°) = 1) and zero when parallel.

Imagine turning a screwdriver. Your hand pushes the handle (vector a) and the blade points in a certain direction (vector b). The cross product tells you which way the screw moves—perpendicular to both your push and the blade orientation.

In Drone Control: Drones are 6D rigid bodies (3 positions, 3 orientations). To rotate a drone, you apply torque, which is the cross product: τ = r × F (position of the force × force vector). Each motor produces a torque about the drone's center; the controller uses cross products to compute the required motor speeds to achieve desired roll, pitch, and yaw rates.

1.4 Matrices — Transformations

A matrix is a rectangular array of numbers. In the context of drones, matrices represent transformations: ways to rotate, scale, or change the coordinate system.

What is a Matrix?

General 3×3 Matrix
M = [m₁₁ m₁₂ m₁₃]
    [m₂₁ m₂₂ m₂₃]
    [m₃₁ m₃₂ m₃₃]

Think of a matrix as a machine that transforms input vectors into output vectors. You put in a vector, multiply by the matrix, and get a transformed vector.

Matrix-Vector Multiplication

Matrix × Vector
M · v = [m₁₁ m₁₂ m₁₃] · [v₁]
        [m₂₁ m₂₂ m₂₃]   [v₂]
        [m₃₁ m₃₂ m₃₃]   [v₃]
= [m₁₁v₁ + m₁₂v₂ + m₁₃v₃]
  [m₂₁v₁ + m₂₂v₂ + m₂₃v₃]
  [m₃₁v₁ + m₃₂v₂ + m₃₃v₃]

Rotation Matrices

The most important matrices for drones are rotation matrices. These rotate a vector by an angle θ around an axis.

Roll rotation (around x-axis by angle φ):

Roll Matrix
Rₓ(φ) = [1 0 0 ]
        [0 cos(φ) -sin(φ)]
        [0 sin(φ) cos(φ)]

Pitch rotation (around y-axis by angle θ):

Pitch Matrix
Rᵧ(θ) = [cos(θ) 0 sin(θ)]
        [ 0 1 0 ]
        [-sin(θ) 0 cos(θ)]

Yaw rotation (around z-axis by angle ψ):

Yaw Matrix
Rz(ψ) = [cos(ψ) -sin(ψ) 0]
        [sin(ψ) cos(ψ) 0]
        [ 0 0 1]

Matrix Multiplication: Chaining Transformations

You can combine multiple rotations by multiplying their matrices:

Chained Rotations
v_rotated = Rz(ψ) · Rᵧ(θ) · Rₓ(φ) · v_original

This applies roll first, then pitch, then yaw.

Identity Matrix

The identity matrix I does nothing to a vector—it's the "do nothing" transformation:

Identity Matrix
I = [1 0 0]
    [0 1 0]
    [0 0 1]
I · v = v

Inverse Matrix: Undoing a Transformation

For a rotation matrix R, the inverse R⁻¹ undoes the rotation:

Matrix Inverse
R⁻¹ · R = I
For rotation matrices: R⁻¹ = Rᵀ (transpose)

A rotation matrix is like a head turn. If you rotate your head 45° to the right, applying the inverse rotation (45° to the left) brings you back to the original orientation.

In Drone Control: The drone body has its own coordinate system (body frame) where forward is the front, right is the side. The world has a fixed coordinate system (earth frame). Rotation matrices convert between them. To tell the drone "fly north in earth frame," you must convert to the drone's body frame using rotation matrices, then command the motors.

1.5 Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors — Stability

An eigenvector of a matrix is a special vector that doesn't change direction when multiplied by the matrix—it only scales. The eigenvalue is the scale factor.

Eigenvalue Equation
M · v = λ · v

where v is an eigenvector and λ is the eigenvalue.

Why Eigenvectors Matter

  • They're special directions where the matrix has a simple effect (just scaling)
  • For stable systems, all eigenvalues have negative real parts (perturbations decay)
  • For unstable systems, some eigenvalues have positive real parts (perturbations grow)

Imagine a spinning basketball. Most vectors get thrown around chaotically by the rotation. But the spin axis (an eigenvector) doesn't change direction—it just gets rotated around itself. That's an eigenvector.

In Swarm Stability: A swarm's collective behavior can be modeled as a system with a Laplacian matrix L. The eigenvalues of L tell you how stable the swarm is. If all eigenvalues (except the smallest) are negative, small disturbances die out and the swarm returns to consensus. This is critical for autonomous swarms: you don't want one drone's error to cause the whole swarm to diverge. Eigenvalue analysis lets you prove stability mathematically before deploying the swarm.

Section 2: Calculus (How Things Change)

Calculus is the mathematics of change. Since drones move, accelerate, and adjust, calculus is absolutely essential. It lets you predict where a drone will be next and how to control it to reach a target.

2.1 Derivatives — Rate of Change

A derivative measures how fast something is changing. If position changes with time, the derivative of position is velocity.

Derivative: Position → Velocity
v(t) = dp/dt

If velocity changes, its derivative is acceleration:

Derivative: Velocity → Acceleration
a(t) = dv/dt = d²p/dt²

Partial Derivatives: Multiple Variables

When a quantity depends on multiple variables (like thrust T depends on motor speeds m₁, m₂, m₃, m₄), we use partial derivatives to see how T changes with respect to each motor:

Partial Derivatives
∂T/∂m₁ = how much thrust changes if you tweak motor 1

Numerical Integration for Drones

In discrete time (sampled at Δt), we approximate derivatives with finite differences:

Discrete Velocity Approximation
v[n] ≈ (p[n] − p[n−1]) / Δt

Your car's speedometer shows the derivative of your odometer—how fast the distance traveled is increasing. Pressing the accelerator changes the throttle, which is the derivative of the throttle (derivative of a derivative).

In Real Drones: The flight controller samples position and velocity at ~50 Hz (every 20 ms). It computes derivatives (using differences) to estimate acceleration. Then it applies a control law that often looks like: u = Kp · e + Kd · de/dt where e is position error and de/dt is velocity error. The Kd term uses the derivative to add damping.

2.2 Integration — Accumulation

Integration is the reverse of differentiation. If you integrate velocity, you get position. If you integrate acceleration, you get velocity.

Integration: Velocity → Position
p(t) = ∫ v(τ) dτ

Numerical Integration: Accumulating Sensor Data

Drones don't measure position directly—they measure acceleration via an accelerometer. To find position, you integrate acceleration twice:

Double Integration for Position
v[n] = v[n−1] + a[n] · Δt
p[n] = p[n−1] + v[n] · Δt

The Drift Problem

Integration has a critical flaw: errors accumulate. If the accelerometer is off by 0.1 m/s² and you integrate for 100 seconds, you've accumulated 10 m/s of velocity error. Integrate that again, and you have 500 m of position error. This is why drones can't rely on accelerometers alone—they need GPS or visual odometry to correct drift.

Imagine filling a bathtub. The water flowing in (flow rate) is like velocity. The total water (amount) is like position. If the faucet's flow rate is measured slightly wrong and you forget to turn it off, you'll overflow the tub—errors accumulate.

In Drone Localization: Early quadcopters used only IMU integration and accumulated massive position drift within seconds. Modern drones fuse GPS, visual odometry, and IMU using a Kalman filter (Section 3.4) to keep integration errors bounded. The Kalman filter's strength is that it uses slow, accurate sensors (GPS) to correct the accumulated errors of fast, noisy integration.

2.3 Gradient — Direction of Steepest Change

The gradient is a generalization of the derivative to multiple dimensions. It's a vector that points in the direction of steepest increase.

Gradient Vector
∇f = [∂f/∂x, ∂f/∂y, ∂f/∂z]

The gradient always points uphill. The negative gradient points downhill.

Gradient in Swarm Path Planning

You can define an artificial potential field where obstacles are peaks (high potential) and the goal is a valley (low potential). Each drone experiences a force proportional to the negative gradient:

Potential Field Force
F = −∇Φ(p)

The drone rolls downhill toward the goal, automatically avoiding obstacles. The gradient tells the drone which way to move to reduce the potential energy fastest.

Interactive: Potential Field & Gradient
Left-click to place drone | Right-click to place goal | Blue = low potential (goal), Red = high potential (obstacles)

Imagine a landscape with mountains (obstacles) and a valley (goal). The gradient points uphill everywhere. The negative gradient points downhill. A ball rolling on this landscape will naturally roll downhill following the negative gradient toward the valley.

In Swarm Navigation: Potential field methods scale to large swarms because each drone computes its own gradient independently based on local sensor readings. No central computation needed. Drones "cooperate" by following the same potential field—they naturally spread out to avoid each other (negative gradient of inter-drone repulsion potential) while approaching the goal (negative gradient of goal attraction potential).

2.4 Differential Equations — Modeling Drone Physics

A differential equation is an equation involving derivatives. It's used to model how a system evolves over time. Drone physics is fundamentally differential equations.

Newton's Second Law as a Differential Equation

Newton's F = ma
F = m · a
F = m · d²p/dt²

For a drone, F is the total force (thrust − gravity − drag). Rearranging:

Drone Acceleration Equation
d²p/dt² = (F_thrust − F_gravity − F_drag) / m

State-Space Representation

Engineers often write systems in state-space form, where the system state x evolves according to input u:

Linear State-Space Form
ẋ = Ax + Bu
y = Cx + Du

For a 1D drone: x = [position, velocity]ᵀ, u = [thrust command]

1D Drone State-Space
d/dt [p] [0 1] [p] [0 ]
        [—] = [— —] [—] + [—-] u
        [v] [0 0] [v] [1/m]

Nonlinearity: Why Drones Are Tricky

The equations above are linear. Real drones are nonlinear. For example, thrust doesn't just push up—it depends on motor speed exponentially. Drag depends on velocity squared. Attitude dynamics involve trigonometric functions.

That's why controllers use gain scheduling or linearization: approximate the nonlinear system as linear around a nominal operating point, design a linear controller, then hope it works when the drone deviates slightly.

Modeling a drone is like predicting the weather. You know the differential equations (Navier-Stokes). But they're nonlinear and sensitive to initial conditions. You solve them numerically to forecast the next hour, but errors grow. Weather forecasts are pretty good for 3 days, terrible beyond a week.

In Drone Firmware: The flight controller solves differential equations numerically in real-time (every 10–20 ms). The inner loop integrates acceleration from the IMU to estimate velocity and position (integration). The outer loop compares desired vs actual states and computes control inputs (feedback). This happens hundreds of times per second, allowing the drone to stabilize despite wind gusts and model errors.

Section 3: Probability & Statistics (Dealing with Uncertainty)

In the real world, sensors are noisy, wind gusts are unpredictable, and obstacles might appear. Drones live in uncertainty. Probability and statistics let you reason about it mathematically.

3.1 Why Probability Matters for Drones

No sensor is perfect. GPS accuracy is typically ±2 meters. Accelerometers drift. Camera images get blurry in low light. A smart drone doesn't trust a single measurement—it assigns it a probability distribution representing uncertainty.

Measurement Uncertainty

  • GPS: Reports position [x, y, z] but has uncertainty (error standard deviation) ~2 m in open sky
  • Barometer: Measures altitude but drifts with temperature and air pressure changes
  • Accelerometer: Noisy and drifts over time. Can't distinguish between acceleration and tilt
  • Magnetometer: Affected by metal objects, magnetic anomalies, and ferrous materials
  • Camera: Can be blurry, has rolling shutter artifacts, and fails in darkness

3.2 Gaussian (Normal) Distribution

Most sensor errors follow a bell curve—a Gaussian distribution. Errors are small (close to zero) most of the time, and rarely large.

Gaussian (Normal) Distribution PDF
f(x) = (1 / (σ√(2π))) · exp(−(x − μ)² / (2σ²))

Two parameters define a Gaussian:

  • Mean μ: the center, where the measurement is most likely to be
  • Variance σ²: how spread out the distribution is (larger σ = more uncertainty)

The 68-95-99.7 Rule

  • 68% of measurements fall within 1σ of the mean
  • 95% fall within 2σ
  • 99.7% fall within 3σ

Example: GPS says your drone is at (x = 100, y = 200) with σ = 2 m. There's a 68% chance the true position is within 2 m, a 95% chance within 4 m.

Multivariate Gaussian: Uncertainty in 3D

In 3D, uncertainty isn't a circle—it's an ellipsoid (stretched sphere). The covariance matrix Σ encodes the shape:

Multivariate Gaussian
f(x) = (1 / √(2π)ⁿ |Σ|) · exp(−½ (x − μ)ᵀ Σ⁻¹ (x − μ))

The covariance matrix tells you how uncertain each dimension is and whether uncertainties are correlated (e.g., if you're uncertain about x, does that make you more or less uncertain about y?).

Imagine throwing darts at a board. Most land near the bullseye, some land farther away, very few land way off. The distribution of dart locations is roughly Gaussian, centered at your aim point.

In Drone Localization: The drone maintains a Gaussian belief about its position: mean = estimated position, covariance = uncertainty ellipsoid. When it gets a GPS measurement, it updates its belief. When the covariance grows (using motion model), it becomes more uncertain. When it gets a measurement, the covariance shrinks (becomes more confident). This is exactly what the Kalman filter does.

3.3 Bayes' Theorem — Updating Beliefs

Bayes' theorem is the mathematical foundation for updating beliefs when you get new information. You start with a prior belief, get a measurement, and update to a posterior belief.

Bayes' Theorem
P(A|B) = P(B|A) · P(A) / P(B)

In the context of drones:

Bayes' Theorem for Localization
P(position | measurement) = P(measurement | position) · P(position) / P(measurement)
Posterior = Likelihood × Prior / Evidence

Practical Example

You're trying to localize a drone. Your prior is "it's somewhere in the test area" (broad uncertainty). Your GPS gives a measurement: "the drone is at (100, 200)" but with uncertainty. The measurement is the likelihood. You multiply: likelihood × prior = posterior. The posterior is your updated belief: "the drone is probably near (100, 200), but considering my prior uncertainty, I'm not 100% sure."

You hear a strange noise at night. Prior belief: "probably the cat" (the cat is in the house, 90% prior). But the sound is too loud for a cat—that's unlikely if it's a cat (low likelihood). You update: "probably not the cat, maybe a break-in." Bayes' theorem formalizes this.

In Sensor Fusion: A drone has multiple sensors: GPS (accurate but slow), IMU (fast but drifts), camera (accurate but only indoors). Bayes' theorem lets you combine them optimally. Each sensor provides a likelihood over position. Your prior is your current belief. You update the posterior and use that as the new prior for the next measurement. This sequential process is the Kalman filter.

3.4 Kalman Filter — The Drone's Brain

The Kalman filter is the standard tool for state estimation in drones. It optimally fuses predictions (from a motion model) and measurements (from sensors) to estimate the true state.

Two Steps: Predict and Update

1. Predict: Use your motion model to predict where the drone will be at the next time step:

Predict Step (a priori)
x̂⁻[n] = A · x̂[n−1] + B · u[n]
P⁻[n] = A · P[n−1] · Aᵀ + Q

2. Update: Get a sensor measurement and correct your prediction:

Update Step (a posteriori)
K[n] = P⁻[n] · Hᵀ / (H · P⁻[n] · Hᵀ + R)
x̂[n] = x̂⁻[n] + K[n] · (z[n] − H · x̂⁻[n])
P[n] = (I − K[n] · H) · P⁻[n]

What Each Variable Means

  • x̂: estimated state (position, velocity, etc.)
  • P: covariance matrix (uncertainty of each state)
  • u: control input (thrust command)
  • z: sensor measurement (GPS, IMU, etc.)
  • A, B, H: system matrices (how state evolves, how measurements relate to state)
  • Q: process noise covariance (uncertainty in the motion model)
  • R: measurement noise covariance (sensor noise)
  • K: Kalman gain (how much to trust prediction vs measurement)

The Kalman Filter's Magic

The Kalman filter doesn't just average predictions and measurements—it weights them by their uncertainty. If the prediction is confident (small P) but the measurement is noisy (large R), it trusts the prediction more. If the prediction drifts (large Q) but the measurement is accurate (small R), it trusts the measurement more. This optimal weighting is why the Kalman filter is optimal for linear Gaussian systems.

Extended Kalman Filter (EKF)

Real drone dynamics are nonlinear (trigonometric functions, squared terms). The Extended Kalman Filter linearizes around the current state and applies the standard filter to the linear approximation. It's not optimal anymore, but it works well in practice.

Interactive: Kalman Filter Fusion (GPS vs Estimate)
Black = true position | Red dots = noisy GPS | Blue line = Kalman filter estimate

GPS Noise (σ): 5

You're tracking a friend's car on a map using two sources: GPS on their phone and watching them drive via video. GPS is usually accurate but occasionally glitches. Video is always correct but requires attention. The Kalman filter says: "If GPS and video agree, great. If they disagree slightly, blend them. If GPS is way off (glitch), trust video more."

In Drone Firmware: The flight controller runs an Extended Kalman Filter to estimate the drone's pose (position and orientation). IMU data (fast, noisy) provides acceleration and angular velocity. GPS data (slow, accurate) corrects drift. The EKF fuses them optimally, producing a smooth, accurate state estimate that can be used for control. Without the Kalman filter, drones would either drift (trusting IMU alone) or jitter (trusting GPS alone).

Section 4: Graph Theory & Topology (Swarm Communication)

A swarm is a network of drones. Graph theory is the mathematics of networks. It lets you reason about information flow, connectivity, and collective behavior.

4.1 Graphs — Who Talks to Whom

A graph consists of vertices (nodes) and edges (connections). In a drone swarm, vertices are drones and edges are communication links.

Graph Notation
G = (V, E)
V = set of vertices (drones)
E = set of edges (communication links)

Adjacency Matrix

An adjacency matrix A describes which drones are connected:

Adjacency Matrix
A[i,j] = 1 if drone i can communicate with drone j
A[i,j] = 0 otherwise

Degree

The degree of a vertex is the number of neighbors it has:

Vertex Degree
degree(i) = Σⱼ A[i,j]

A drone with high degree has many neighbors and is well-connected. A drone with degree 1 is on the periphery.

Think of a social network. Vertices are people, edges are friendships. Your degree is the number of friends you have. A well-connected node (high degree) can spread information quickly to the whole network.

4.2 Connectivity — Can Information Reach Everyone?

A graph is connected if information can flow from any vertex to any other (directly or through intermediate vertices). For a swarm, this means all drones can share information, directly or indirectly.

Algebraic Connectivity

The algebraic connectivity (Fiedler value) is the second-smallest eigenvalue λ₂ of the Laplacian matrix L (defined below). It measures "how connected" the graph is:

  • λ₂ > 0: graph is connected
  • λ₂ = 0: graph is disconnected (one or more isolated components)
  • λ₂ large: graph is very strongly connected (many redundant paths)

In Swarm Monitoring: A drone can periodically compute λ₂ of the swarm's connectivity graph using local measurements. If λ₂ is dropping, the swarm is fragmenting. A drone can decide to move closer to neighbors to increase λ₂ and prevent partition. This is a decentralized way to maintain swarm cohesion.

4.3 Laplacian Matrix — The Heart of Consensus

The Laplacian matrix L is defined as L = D − A, where D is the degree matrix (diagonal matrix with D[i,i] = degree of node i).

Laplacian Matrix
L = D − A

Consensus Protocol

With the Laplacian, you can define a simple consensus rule where each drone adjusts its value toward the average of its neighbors' values:

Consensus Update Law
ẋᵢ = Σⱼ (xⱼ − xᵢ) for all neighbors j of i
or in matrix form:
ẋ = −L · x

Convergence to Consensus

If the graph is connected and undirected, this simple rule makes all drones converge to the same value (consensus). The final consensus value is the average of all initial values. The convergence speed depends on λ₂ (the algebraic connectivity)—larger λ₂ means faster convergence.

Example use cases:

  • Temperature regulation: Each drone measures local temperature, and they converge to the average temperature of the environment
  • Time synchronization: Drones adjust their clocks to reach the same time
  • Rendezvous: Drones converge to a common meeting point (using position consensus)
Interactive: Consensus Protocol
Watch drones (nodes) exchange values with neighbors. Color = value. Edges = communication. All converge to same color (consensus).

Imagine a group of people standing in a circle, each with a different body temperature. Periodically, each person adjusts their temperature slightly toward the average of their neighbors' temperatures. Over time, everyone converges to the same temperature—consensus achieved.

In Swarm Control: The consensus protocol is the foundation of many distributed swarm algorithms. For example, flocking: each drone's heading is the average heading of neighbors → all drones align. Swarming: each drone's position drifts toward the average position of neighbors → all drones converge to a center. The Laplacian captures all of this mathematically, making it possible to prove convergence and analyze stability.

4.4 Formation Control via Graph Theory

Instead of consensus (all drones converge to the same value), you might want them to maintain specific relative distances—a formation.

Desired Formation as a Graph

A formation is defined by the desired inter-drone distances. You can represent this as a graph where edge weights are desired distances:

Distance-based Formation
For each edge (i, j): desired distance dᵢⱼ

Each drone tries to maintain this distance from its neighbors:

Distance Control Law
Force on drone i = Σⱼ (k · (||pⱼ − pᵢ|| − dᵢⱼ) · (pⱼ − pᵢ) / ||pⱼ − pᵢ||)

Each drone pulls/pushes based on the difference between current and desired distance to each neighbor.

Rigidity Theory

A formation is rigid if once the distances are satisfied, the shape is fixed (can't deform by moving drones while keeping distances the same). Rigidity is important because it ensures the formation doesn't collapse or expand unexpectedly.

Bearing-based Control

Instead of maintaining distances, you can maintain angles (bearings) between drones. This is sometimes more robust because it uses relative direction rather than absolute distance (which requires ranging sensors). The math involves unit vectors:

Bearing Vector
bᵢⱼ = (pⱼ − pᵢ) / ||pⱼ − pᵢ|| (unit vector from i to j)

In Swarm Surveillance: A swarm of 6 drones might maintain a hexagonal formation (each drone equidistant from 2 neighbors). Using distance control laws derived from graph Laplacian theory, the swarm self-organizes into this formation. If one drone is perturbed by wind, the control laws restore the formation. If one drone fails, the formation adapts (becomes a pentagon). This self-healing property emerges from the mathematics of graph-based formation control.

Section 5: Optimization (Finding the Best Solution)

Drones operate in constrained environments. They have limited battery, can't exceed speed limits, must avoid obstacles. Optimization is the mathematics of finding the best solution within constraints.

5.1 What is Optimization?

Optimization means finding the input that minimizes (or maximizes) a cost function J(x) subject to constraints.

Optimization Problem
minimize J(x)
subject to:
  g(x) ≤ 0 (inequality constraints)
  h(x) = 0 (equality constraints)
  x ∈ [lower, upper] (box constraints)

Example: Drone Path Planning

  • Objective: minimize path length (or time to goal, or energy use)
  • Constraints:
    • avoid obstacles (inequality constraints: distance to obstacle > 0)
    • start at initial position, end at goal (equality constraints)
    • stay within speed limits (box constraints: velocity ≤ v_max)

Planning a road trip: minimize driving time (objective) while staying on roads (constraint) and obeying speed limits (constraint). Not all solutions are valid, and among valid solutions, the optimal one is fastest.

5.2 Convex vs Non-Convex

A convex optimization problem has a single global minimum—you're guaranteed to find it. A non-convex problem has multiple local minima—you might get stuck at a suboptimal solution.

Convex Problem

The objective function J(x) is convex if for any two points x₁, x₂, the line segment between them lies above the curve:

Convexity Condition
J(λx₁ + (1−λ)x₂) ≤ λJ(x₁) + (1−λ)J(x₂) for λ ∈ [0, 1]

Convex problems: linear programming, quadratic programming (if the quadratic is positive semidefinite), least-squares optimization.

Non-Convex Problem

Drone path planning is usually non-convex because obstacles create multiple valleys (local minima). You can get stuck in one valley and never reach the true global optimum.

  • Solutions:
    • Random restart: solve from multiple initial guesses, pick the best
    • Convex relaxation: find a convex approximation, solve it, hope it's close
    • Heuristics: genetic algorithms, simulated annealing, particle swarm optimization
    • Sampling-based methods: RRT*, RRT-Connect (for path planning)

In a bowl (convex), rolling a ball always leads to the bottom. In mountains (non-convex), a ball might get stuck in a valley that's not the lowest point on Earth. You might have to restart from a different position or throw the ball harder to escape.

5.3 Linear Programming

Linear programming optimizes a linear objective with linear constraints. It's solved efficiently by the simplex method.

Linear Programming
minimize cᵀx
subject to Ax ≤ b, x ≥ 0

Task Allocation in Swarms

Suppose you have N drones and M targets. Each drone can service multiple targets (with a cost), and each target must be serviced by exactly one drone. Find the assignment that minimizes total cost:

Task Allocation Cost
minimize Σᵢ Σⱼ costᵢⱼ · xᵢⱼ
subject to:
  Σᵢ xᵢⱼ = 1 (each target assigned to exactly one drone)
  xᵢⱼ ∈ {0, 1}

This is a linear assignment problem—one of the few task allocation problems solvable in polynomial time (Hungarian algorithm).

5.4 Quadratic Programming

Quadratic programming has a quadratic objective and linear constraints. Common in control and trajectory optimization.

Quadratic Programming
minimize ½ xᵀ Q x + cᵀ x
subject to Ax ≤ b, x ≥ 0

Minimum-Jerk Trajectory

A common drone trajectory objective is minimum jerk (third derivative of position, or rate of change of acceleration). Smooth trajectories put less stress on actuators:

Minimum-Jerk Cost
J = ∫ ||d³p/dt³||² dt

This is a quadratic functional—can be discretized into a QP problem and solved efficiently.

In Drone Motion Planning: A trajectory planning algorithm finds a smooth path from start to goal while minimizing energy use or smoothness. QP solvers can generate these trajectories in milliseconds, fast enough for real-time control.

5.5 Mixed-Integer Programming

Some variables are continuous (like position), some are discrete (like which drone goes where). Mixed-integer programming (MIP) handles this.

Mixed-Integer Programming
minimize J(x_continuous, x_integer)
subject to constraints
x_integer ∈ ℤ (integers)

Combinatorial Task Assignment

Assign N drones to M targets, minimizing total distance:

Combinatorial Assignment
x[i,j] ∈ {0, 1} (binary decision: does drone i go to target j?)

This is NP-hard for general problems, but for small swarms (N, M < 10), MIP solvers work fine.

5.6 Gradient Descent & Variants

Gradient descent is an iterative algorithm that takes steps downhill (in the direction of negative gradient) to find a local minimum.

Gradient Descent Update
x_new = x_old − α · ∇J(x_old)

The learning rate α controls step size: too big = overshoot, too small = slow convergence.

Variants for Better Convergence

  • Momentum: accumulate past gradient direction for faster convergence
  • Adam (Adaptive Moment Estimation): adaptive learning rates per parameter
  • RMSprop: adaptive learning rate using historical squared gradients

Decentralized Gradient Descent for Swarms

Each drone computes a local gradient (using local sensors) and updates its position in the negative gradient direction. If gradients are shared among neighbors, the swarm collectively finds the global optimum:

Decentralized Gradient Descent
x_i_new = x_i_old − α · ∇Jᵢ(x_i_old) + Σⱼ consensus_term

In Swarm Learning: If each drone has a local cost function (e.g., "how far am I from neighbors?") and they run decentralized gradient descent with consensus, the swarm collectively optimizes a global cost (e.g., "minimize energy while maintaining formation"). Deep reinforcement learning (DRL) for swarms often uses variants of gradient descent (Adam) to train neural network policies for distributed control.
