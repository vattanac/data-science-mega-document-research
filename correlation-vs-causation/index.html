Correlation vs Causation

Understanding the difference that matters in data science

📊 What Is Correlation?

Correlation measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two variables. When two variables move together in a predictable pattern, they are said to be correlated.

Pearson Correlation Coefficient (r)

The most common measure of correlation is Pearson's r, which quantifies how closely two variables follow a linear relationship.

r = Σ[(x - x̄)(y - ȳ)] / √[Σ(x - x̄)² × Σ(y - ȳ)²]

Interpretation Scale

r = 1

Perfect positive correlation - variables move together perfectly

0.7 to 0.9

Strong positive correlation - strong relationship

0.3 to 0.7

Moderate correlation - reasonable relationship

0 to 0.3

Weak correlation - minimal relationship

r = 0

No correlation - variables independent

r = -1

Perfect negative correlation - inverse relationship

Key Point: Correlation ranges from -1 to 1. The sign tells direction (positive vs negative), the magnitude tells strength. Correlation only captures linear relationships.

⚡ What Is Causation?

Causation means that changes in one variable directly cause changes in another variable. There's a mechanism or process by which one variable influences the other.

Characteristics of Causation

  • Mechanism: There's a clear explanation for why one thing causes another
  • Temporal Order: The cause must occur before the effect
  • Controlled Environment: Relationships hold true when other factors are controlled
  • Reproducibility: The effect can be replicated in multiple contexts

Establishing Causation

The gold standard for establishing causation is the randomized controlled experiment:

  • Randomly assign subjects to treatment and control groups
  • Apply the treatment to the treatment group only
  • Compare outcomes between groups
  • Any differences can be attributed to the treatment
Bradford Hill Criteria: Strength of association, consistency, specificity, temporality, biological gradient, plausibility, coherence, experiment, and analogy help establish causation.

🔄 Why Correlation ≠ Causation

Just because two variables move together doesn't mean one causes the other. There are several reasons why correlation can occur without causation:

1. Confounding Variables

A third variable causes changes in both the variables you're observing, creating a spurious correlation.

Example: Ice cream sales correlate with drowning deaths, but a confounding variable—summer weather—causes both to increase independently.

2. Reverse Causation

You might think X causes Y, but actually Y causes X, or both cause each other in a feedback loop.

3. Selection Bias

Your sample selection might create spurious correlations that don't exist in the broader population.

4. Coincidence

With enough variables, some will correlate by pure chance. This is why multiple hypothesis testing requires corrections.

Famous Spurious Correlations

Nicolas Cage Films & Drowning Deaths

Strong positive correlation, zero causation. Pure coincidence of timing.

Shoe Size & Reading Ability (Children)

Correlated because age confounds both. Older children have bigger feet AND read better.

Coffee Consumption & Heart Disease

Early studies showed correlation, but confounders (stress, smoking) caused both, not the coffee.

Low Cholesterol & Mortality

Correlation exists, but causation is reversed: illness lowers cholesterol as people get sicker.

The Key Insight: Correlation is necessary for causation, but not sufficient. You need correlation AND evidence ruling out alternative explanations.

🔀 Simpson's Paradox

Simpson's Paradox is a phenomenon where a trend appears in aggregated data but reverses when the data is broken down by subgroups. It's a powerful example of why understanding data requires careful analysis.

Classic Example: Berkeley Admissions (1973)

Overall, the University of California at Berkeley appeared to discriminate against female applicants. However, when analyzing by department, most departments actually had higher acceptance rates for women.

What Happened: Women applied disproportionately to competitive departments with lower acceptance rates for everyone. Men applied more to less competitive departments with higher acceptance rates. The confounding variable was the department, not gender bias.

Why This Matters

  • Always disaggregate your data by relevant subgroups
  • Correlations at aggregate level may not hold at granular level
  • Be cautious of causal claims based on aggregate data alone
  • Identify potential confounders and stratify by them
Aggregate Trend ≠ Trend Within Each Subgroup

💡 Why It Matters in Data Science

Understanding correlation vs causation is critical for responsible data science practice:

Feature Selection & Model Building

Including highly correlated features might improve prediction accuracy, but it doesn't mean those features cause the outcome. This matters for interpretability and generalization.

Lesson: A feature correlated with your target in training data might not be available or predictive in production if the causal relationship doesn't exist.

A/B Testing

Randomized experiments (A/B tests) establish causation by eliminating confounders. This is why they're the gold standard for testing product changes.

Causal Inference Techniques

When randomized experiments aren't possible, techniques like propensity score matching, instrumental variables, and difference-in-differences help estimate causal effects from observational data.

Business Impact

  • Prediction: You only need correlation (does feature predict outcome?)
  • Explanation: You need causation (does feature cause outcome?)
  • Action: Only causal relationships guide effective interventions
  • Risk: Acting on spurious correlations can waste resources or cause harm
Rule of Thumb: For prediction tasks, correlation is enough. For causal decisions (policy, intervention, marketing), you need evidence of causation.
