1. What Is a Probability Distribution?

A probability distribution describes how likely each possible outcome is. It's a complete "recipe" for randomness — it tells you the probability of every possible event.

Analogy: A Loaded Die

A fair die has a uniform distribution: each face (1-6) has probability 1/6. A loaded die has a different distribution — maybe face 6 has probability 1/3 while others share the rest. The distribution is the complete description of "how unfair" the die is. Once you know the distribution, you know everything about the randomness.

Every distribution has key properties:

Mean (μ)

The "center of gravity" — the expected average outcome if you repeated the experiment forever.

Variance (σ²)

How spread out the outcomes are. Low variance = clustered around the mean. High variance = wide spread.

PDF / PMF

PDF (continuous) or PMF (discrete): the function that gives the probability (or density) at each point.

CDF

Cumulative Distribution Function: P(X ≤ x). Starts at 0, ends at 1. Answers "what's the probability of getting at most x?"

2. Discrete vs Continuous

Discrete

Countable outcomes (0, 1, 2, 3, ...). Uses a PMF (probability mass function). Probabilities are actual numbers that sum to 1.

Examples: coin flips, dice rolls, number of website visits, defect counts.

Continuous

Uncountably many outcomes on a continuum. Uses a PDF (probability density function). Individual point probabilities are 0; only intervals have nonzero probability.

Examples: height, temperature, time between events, stock prices.

Key Distinction

For discrete distributions, P(X = 5) can be 0.3 (a real number). For continuous distributions, P(X = 5.000...) = 0 always — you must ask P(4.9 < X < 5.1) instead. The PDF gives density, not probability; you must integrate to get probability.

4. Uniform Distribution

Discrete Uniform

Each of n outcomes equally likely. P(X = k) = 1/n.

Example: Rolling a fair die (n=6), each face has P = 1/6.

Mean: (a+b)/2   Var: (n²−1)/12

Continuous Uniform

Constant density on interval [a, b]. PDF = 1/(b−a).

Example: Random number generators, bus arrival within a window.

Mean: (a+b)/2   Var: (b−a)²/12

When to Use

Use uniform when you have no reason to prefer any outcome over another. It's the "maximum ignorance" distribution — the mathematical way of saying "I have absolutely no idea, any value is equally likely."

5. Bernoulli & Binomial

Bernoulli(p) — A Single Yes/No Trial

The simplest distribution: one trial, two outcomes (success with probability p, failure with probability 1−p).

Bernoulli PMF
P(X = 1) = p,   P(X = 0) = 1 − p   |   Mean = p,   Var = p(1−p)

Binomial(n, p) — Count of Successes in n Trials

Repeat n independent Bernoulli trials. X = total number of successes.

Binomial PMF
P(X = k) = C(n,k) · p^k · (1−p)^(n−k)   |   Mean = np,   Var = np(1−p)
Real-World Examples

A/B testing: n=1000 visitors, p=0.05 conversion rate. How many conversions? Binomial!
Quality control: n=100 items, p=0.02 defect rate. How many defects? Binomial!
Surveys: n=500 respondents, p = proportion who say "yes". Count of "yes" answers is Binomial.

6. Poisson Distribution

Poisson(λ) — Count of Events in a Fixed Interval

Models the number of events occurring in a fixed time/space interval, when events happen independently at a constant average rate λ.

Poisson PMF
P(X = k) = (λ^k · e^(−λ)) / k!   |   Mean = λ,   Var = λ

A special property: the mean equals the variance. If your count data has mean ≈ variance, Poisson is a good fit.

Real-World Examples

Website traffic: Average 50 visits/hour → Poisson(λ=50).
Rare diseases: 3 cases per year in a city → Poisson(λ=3).
Typos: Average 2 typos per page → how many typos on a given page?
Call center: 10 calls per minute → how many in the next minute?

Poisson as Binomial Limit

When n is large and p is small (many trials, rare events), Binomial(n,p) ≈ Poisson(λ=np). This is why Poisson is called the "law of rare events."

7. Geometric & Negative Binomial

Geometric(p) — Trials Until First Success

Keep flipping a coin until you get heads. X = number of trials needed.

Geometric PMF
P(X = k) = (1−p)^(k−1) · p   |   Mean = 1/p,   Var = (1−p)/p²

Memoryless property: Geometric is the only discrete distribution with this. No matter how many failures so far, the probability of success on the next trial is still p. "The coin doesn't remember."

Negative Binomial(r, p) — Trials Until r Successes

Generalization: wait for r-th success instead of 1st. Mean = r/p.

Use case: Modeling overdispersed count data (when variance > mean, unlike Poisson where they're equal).

8. Normal (Gaussian) Distribution

Normal(μ, σ²) — The "Bell Curve"

The most important distribution in all of statistics. Appears everywhere due to the Central Limit Theorem.

Normal PDF
f(x) = (1 / σ√(2π)) · exp(−(x−μ)² / 2σ²)
Why It's Everywhere

Central Limit Theorem: The average of many independent random variables (regardless of their individual distributions) tends toward a normal distribution. This is why heights, test scores, measurement errors, stock returns, and countless natural phenomena follow bell curves — they're the sum of many small random effects.

The 68-95-99.7 Rule (Empirical Rule)

• 68% of data falls within ±1σ of the mean

• 95% of data falls within ±2σ of the mean

• 99.7% of data falls within ±3σ of the mean

Standard Normal Z ~ N(0,1)

Any normal can be standardized: Z = (X − μ) / σ. Z-scores tell you "how many standard deviations away from the mean." This is how p-values, confidence intervals, and hypothesis tests work.

10. Exponential Distribution

Exponential(λ) — Time Between Events

If events occur at a Poisson rate λ, the time between consecutive events follows an Exponential distribution. It's the continuous counterpart of the Geometric distribution.

Exponential PDF
f(x) = λ · e^(−λx) for x ≥ 0   |   Mean = 1/λ,   Var = 1/λ²

Memoryless property: P(X > s+t | X > s) = P(X > t). The remaining wait time doesn't depend on how long you've already waited. This makes it ideal for modeling lifetimes of "ageless" components.

Real-World Examples

Time between customer arrivals (λ = 5 per hour → mean wait = 12 min).
Radioactive decay (time until next emission).
Server requests (time between API calls).
Light bulb lifetime (memoryless = no "wear out").

11. Beta Distribution

Beta(α, β) — Distribution of Probabilities

Defined on [0, 1] — perfect for modeling probabilities, proportions, and rates. It's the "distribution over distributions."

Beta PDF
f(x) = x^(α−1) · (1−x)^(β−1) / B(α,β)   |   Mean = α/(α+β)

The shape depends entirely on α and β:

• α = β = 1: Uniform on [0,1] (no information)

• α = β > 1: Symmetric bell curve centered at 0.5

• α > β: Skewed right (favors higher values)

• α < β: Skewed left (favors lower values)

• α, β < 1: U-shaped (extreme values more likely)

The Bayesian Superpower

Beta is the conjugate prior for the Binomial. Start with Beta(α, β) as your prior belief about a probability. Observe s successes and f failures. Your posterior is Beta(α+s, β+f). This makes Bayesian updating trivially easy!

Example: You think a coin is fair → Beta(10, 10). You flip 7 heads, 3 tails. Posterior: Beta(17, 13). Mean shifts from 0.5 to 0.567 — slightly toward heads.

13. Gamma & Chi-Squared

Gamma(α, β)

Generalizes the Exponential. Models the time until the α-th event in a Poisson process. When α=1, it's Exponential.

Mean: α/β   Var: α/β²

Use: Insurance claims, rainfall amounts, queuing times.

Chi-Squared(k)

Sum of k squared standard normals: χ² = Z₁² + Z₂² + ... + Zₖ². It's a special case of Gamma(k/2, 1/2).

Mean: k   Var: 2k

Use: Goodness-of-fit tests, confidence intervals for variance, contingency tables.

Quick Decision Flowchart

Is your data discrete or continuous?
→ Discrete: Is it binary? (Bernoulli/Binomial) Is it count data? (Poisson if mean≈var, Negative Binomial if var>mean)
→ Continuous: Is it always positive? (Exponential/Gamma) Is it on [0,1]? (Beta) Could it be negative? (Normal)

The Big Picture

These distributions are the building blocks of all statistical modeling. Every ML model, every hypothesis test, every confidence interval assumes some distribution. Understanding which distribution to use — and why — is one of the most valuable skills in data science.
